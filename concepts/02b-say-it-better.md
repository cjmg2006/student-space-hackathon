# Concept 02b — Say It Better

A voice-rant-to-articulation tool. Students talk through whatever they want to articulate. The app gives them back their own meaning, more clearly, in a few drafts. They pick the one that feels true. Over time, a personal language library accumulates and compounds: a private dictionary of self that the student can search, reuse, and revise across years.

## Anchor

**Primary hypotheses:** H1 (linguistic gap), H5 (past launchpad experiences), H10 (fear of consequences), H11 (agency over surface).

**Supporting hypotheses:** H2 (outward easier than inward), H9 (trust individuals not institutions).

**Tangential connections:** H3 (hard to see what's possible), H4 (friends shape self-knowledge), H12 (action gap).

**Live tensions:** H6 (students are relational; platform alone won't move them) — 02b is solo by design and must be deliberately positioned to avoid the Journey Together failure mode.

## The strategic bet (sharpened)

The original framing: "the articulation gap is the language gap; close it directly."

The deeper framing the connection map exposes: **naming is the upstream act of identity.** Students who can name what they perceive can examine, share, compare, act, and evolve. Students who can't, can't. Self-perception, self-advocacy, decision-making, identity formation, even emotional regulation are all downstream of self-articulation. The research's most-cited gap (linguistic, not experiential) is structural to almost every other gap surfaced.

02b is not a writing tool. It is **self-perception infrastructure**: a private mechanism for converting raw inner experience into language the student recognizes as their own. The library is the student's own dictionary of self. Build the language; the perception, advocacy, and continuity follow.

## Pitch

Voice-rant about anything. The app gives you back your own words, more articulate. Pick the version that feels true. Build a library of your language for yourself, ready when you need it.

## Connection map

This is the centerpiece. Every research insight, hypothesis, pedagogical framework, and stakeholder finding that 02b plausibly touches, with strength of connection and the specific quote or evidence.

### Hypotheses (H1–H12)

**H1 — The gap is linguistic, not experiential.** Strength: **PRIMARY**.
> "Less ready students give very surface level responses: I feel good, I do this, without analysing why." Even high-achieving students lack "coherence." (Apr 21)

The whole concept is built to attack this. The "three versions" output is the language scaffolding moment. Surface responses ("I feel good, I do this") get returned as three articulated drafts, and the student picks or revises toward truth.

**H2 — Students see their journey outward (pathways) more easily than inward (self).** Strength: **SUPPORTING**.
> "Students talk more about their journey (pathways) more easily than their world (internal self-understanding). Can't connect world of work, with who they are, with their education." (Apr 22 ECG stakeholder)

Version 2 in the three-version output is explicitly an **identity claim** ("I'm someone who..."). That version is the inward-bridge prompt. The student rants outward (events, what happened); the AI returns options that include an inward-naming version. Repeated use of this version trains the student to name the inward signal in their own outward experiences.

**H3 — Students find it hard to see what's possible for them.** Strength: **TANGENTIAL but real**.
> "Some are really fighting for survival, they will not be thinking so far ahead. They can't relate to it, or see a future for themselves." (Apr 23)

02b doesn't generate possibilities (that's 03 Future Self Simulator). But: when a student articulates "I'm someone who can be relied on when no one's watching," the articulation itself is a small expansion of their self-image. Identity claims compound over time. The library, read back at week 12, reveals selves the student didn't know they were becoming. Possibility expansion happens as a side effect of naming.

**H4 — Friends shape self-knowledge more than formal inputs.** Strength: **TANGENTIAL** (but supports H4 dynamics).
> "40 to 50% of student issues surface through friends rather than direct teacher contact." (Apr 21)

02b is solo, not peer. But the library is rehearsal infrastructure for friend conversations. When a friend asks "what kind of person are you?" or "why did you do that?", students with a library have an answer ready that's actually theirs. Articulations can also be shared into friend conversations as quotes ("I wrote this last week, listen"). 02b feeds H4 without enacting it.

**H5 — Past launchpad experiences.** Strength: **PRIMARY**.
> "Experiences are indeed a powerful leaping point, even if level of maturity is: I like X, I'm good at Y. The intervention mines and connects." (Apr 22)

Every rant is about a past experience. The tool turns thin past beliefs ("I'm good at organizing birthday parties" — Esther's example) into articulated identity claims that become foundations for future action. The library is the student's accumulated launchpad collection.

**H6 — Students are relational; platform alone won't move them.** Strength: **CRITICAL TENSION**.
> "Apr 23 SkillsFuture portal: a small proportion of students took it seriously, it feels like a task. Journey Together reflection feature: near-zero usage." (Apr 14)

02b is a standalone digital tool. This is the failure mode for prior MOE attempts. Mitigations: (1) introduction by a trusted human (teacher, peer, parent, sibling) at first contact; (2) library entries shareable by the student into real conversations, so the tool extends rather than replaces relationships; (3) explicit framing as a personal utility, not an institutional reflection layer; (4) low-stakes entry — no streaks, no goals, just "say it better when you want to."

**H9 — Students trust individuals, not institutions.** Strength: **SUPPORTING**.
> "Students only want to speak with people they're comfortable with, familiar faces preferred over professional services." (Apr 21)

The AI is positioned as a private utility, not an institutional voice. No teacher account. No school server. Provider posture is visible in-app at all times. Trust depends on the AI feeling like a personal tool, not a wellbeing-team-with-a-rephrase-button. When the AI rephrases, it speaks back in the student's own register, not in counsellor or institutional voice.

**H10 — Authentic sharing is suppressed by fear of consequences.** Strength: **PRIMARY (as mitigation)**.
> Ang Yu Xian: "I said all these things and suddenly you drag me to counselling, call my parents." (Apr 21)
> "As a student I'm not sure if I'll write it if it's an MOE diary." (Apr 20)

This is the load-bearing tension across the whole project. 02b's solo, no-audience design is its biggest strength here. There is no recipient on the other side of a rant. The AI rephrases and forgets. Only the saved articulation persists, owned by the student, never auto-shared. Ang Yu Xian's specific failure mode (disclosure leads to escalation) is structurally impossible: no human is reading.

This positions 02b as the **first step** in a disclosure ladder. A student who can't yet talk to a counsellor, a teacher, a friend, or even themselves can rant at the AI. The library accumulates. Over time, the student may choose to share an articulation with a person of their choice, on their terms. That's a different decision from being heard in real time.

**H11 — Agency over surface matters.** Strength: **PRIMARY**.
> "ETC can choose whether it is a physical thing, Google doc, Notion etc., encouraged to shape it however they prefer, a surface they need to enjoy." Students who feel ownership take it seriously.

02b is the most agentic of the three concepts. The student rants in their own raw language. The AI offers three drafts. The student picks, edits, tags with their own taxonomy, organizes, searches. Every output is theirs. There is no institutional template imposed on the surface.

**H12 — Action gap.** Strength: **TANGENTIAL** (but real).
> "Instead of figuring out 'I want to be a doctor', how about helping them find the next smallest thing they can try? Connecting 'you want to be an influencer' to 'you need to learn to write scripts and understand data'." (Apr 21)

02b is articulation, not action. But: articulating a problem in clear language is often the precursor to acting on it. "I keep doubting my first instinct" is a different prompt for action than "I lost marks." A future feature could surface "what's the smallest thing this articulation suggests you might try?" but that risks scope creep into 03 Simulator territory. For now, the action gap is bridged indirectly: the library makes problems and patterns nameable, and named problems are easier to address.

### Specific research findings beyond the H-numbered hypotheses

**The surface-response problem.**
> "I feel good, I do this, without analysing why." (Apr 21)

This phrase IS the input pattern 02b is built for. A student who only has "I feel good, I do this" gets back three drafts that include the missing "why," in their own register. The student edits toward what's actually true.

**The coherence problem.**
> "Even high-achieving students lack coherence." (Apr 21)

Coherence is not a single articulation; it's the relationship between articulations over time. The library compounds. Search, sort, and pattern-detection across saved entries builds coherence as a byproduct. A student who has 30 saved articulations and notices "I keep coming back to being underestimated" has built coherence without anyone teaching it.

**The Sec 1 trust gap.**
> "Takes time to build trust, students very reserved at start of Sec 1 or when teacher doesn't know them. More open from Term 2 onwards." (Apr 21)

02b doesn't require trust in a person. The AI is a neutral tool. This makes it potentially valuable as an entry point for new students who don't yet have a trusted adult or peer to confide in. Articulations built in Sec 1 carry forward. By the time the student finds a trusted teacher in Term 2 or Sec 2, they have language ready.

**The "ETC chooses the surface" finding (H11 evidence, deepened).**
> Teachers noted students who curate their own e-portfolio (in Notion, Google Doc, physical scrapbook) take it seriously.

02b sits naturally inside this finding. The student didn't choose 02b's interface; they chose to USE it. The artifact (the saved articulation) can be exported, copy-pasted, or shared into the student's own preferred surface. 02b is a generator, not a container. It feeds whatever surface the student already enjoys.

**The performative response problem.**
> Journey Together: near-zero usage. SkillsFuture portal: "feels like a task."

The structural cause of performative responses is being read by an institution. 02b removes the institutional reader. There is no audience to perform for. The only judge is the student's own ear ("does this feel true?").

**The "you want to be an influencer → write scripts" finding (H12 evidence, deepened).**
> Effective ECG conversations move from reflection to micro-action.

02b can support but doesn't enact this. A student who articulates "I love it when I make people laugh in class" has named something specific. A teacher in a 1:1 can connect that to "have you written a script before?" The articulation makes the connection possible. The action still requires a human.

### Pedagogical frameworks (from research-notes.md)

**Marcia's identity statuses.** 02b explicitly supports **moratorium** — active exploration without premature commitment. Three-version output prevents foreclosure (single answer, locked in). Library evolution over time prevents diffusion (no exploration). Students stay in productive moratorium with language to navigate it.

**McAdams narrative identity.** Identity is a story being constructed. The library is the raw material. Articulations chain into narrative when read in sequence. Cross-articulation patterns ("I keep coming back to X") are exactly the integrating moves McAdams describes.

**Pennebaker expressive writing.** Structured prompts beat blank pages. Three-version output is itself a structured response: the student gets options to react to, not a blank page to fill. The Pennebaker mechanism (coherence-seeking, not venting) is built in.

**Implementation intentions (Gollwitzer).** Tangential. 02b doesn't capture if-then commitments by default, but a saved articulation can become the "if" of a future implementation intention. ("If I notice myself doubting my first instinct again, then I'll write it down before second-guessing.")

**Peer-mediated reflection.** 02b is solo, not peer. But it explicitly outputs language that's quotable into peer conversation. It's a feeder for the peer mechanic in Concept 01 Mirror Talk.

### Second-order problems 02b incidentally addresses

These are downstream effects of the language gap. None of these is the primary frame, but they explain why the connection map is wider than three hypotheses.

1. **Self-perception.** You can't see clearly what you can't say clearly. Library = mirror.
2. **Self-advocacy with adults.** Students who can articulate their experience in adult-legible terms can advocate for themselves to parents, teachers, counsellors. Without language, advocacy collapses into "I dunno" or silent compliance.
3. **Emotional literacy.** "I feel good" → "I feel good when X happens because Y about me responds to it." That progression is the literacy gain.
4. **The quiet-student-with-rich-inner-life pattern.** Introverted or shy students who think a lot but don't speak. Voice-rant is a private outlet that doesn't require a person.
5. **"I have things to say but no one to say them to."** Validating inner experience without requiring an audience reduces a specific form of teen loneliness.
6. **Application contexts (interviews, personal statements, scholarship).** Out of project scope, but the library is exactly what students reach for in those moments. Useful as side-effect, never as frame.
7. **Compounding language deficit across years.** A student who develops articulation habits in Sec 1 has years of compounding self-knowledge by Sec 4. A student who doesn't has compounding deficit.
8. **Cultural / multilingual articulation.** Many Singapore students think in Mandarin, Malay, Tamil, or Singlish. Articulating in code-switched language is more honest than translated-into-standard-English. 02b can preserve this if prompt-tuning is careful.
9. **Decision support without therapy framing.** Students facing subject combos, JC vs poly, leadership elections, friend conflicts can rant and articulate without crossing into "are you OK?" territory. 02b is decision-adjacent without being therapy-adjacent.
10. **Memory as identity scaffolding.** Articulations preserved across years protect against the "I don't remember who I was at 14" gap that adults often regret.

## How it resolves each tension

Updated to reflect the deeper view.

- **Risk 1 (consequence fear, critical).** The strongest of the three concepts on this dimension. No human reads. AI doesn't retain. Library private to student. Sharing is always student-initiated. Ang Yu Xian's specific fear is structurally impossible.
- **Risk 2 (covertness paradox).** Honest framing. "Help me find better words for what I want to say." No reflection-labelling. The mechanism is articulation; reflection is the byproduct.
- **Risk 3 (platform vs relationship, live tension).** This is where 02b is exposed. Mitigations: human introduction at first contact, library that feeds real conversations, explicit positioning as utility not platform, low-stakes commitment. Not bulletproof. See "Failure modes" below.
- **Risk 4 (reactive vs reflective muscles).** Bursty, on-demand. Reactive in the best sense: the student uses it when language is needed. Pairs with Concept 01's high-frequency rhythm and Concept 03's milestone cadence.
- **Risk 5 (teacher as guide vs risk).** Teacher never sees content. Teacher's role is at most introducing the tool. Even in optional sharing, only the student's chosen articulation is visible, not the rant or the rejected versions.
- **Risk 6 (institutional frame).** Outside MOE servers. Student-installed. AI provider contract visible. Critical: NOT badged as a LEAPS or CCE tool.

## Pitch as scenario, across time

**The single session (Joshua, post-interview).**

Joshua, Sec 4, just got back from a JC interview. He felt off. He couldn't say why. He opens the app. Picks "say it better."

Voice-rant: "I think I screwed up the interview. They asked me what I did in CCA and I said I was the equipment manager. They asked what I learned and I said... I just said 'a lot of responsibility.' I felt stupid. Like I had nothing real to say. But I DID stuff. I just couldn't... I dunno."

He taps stop. The app gives him three rephrase options:

> **Version 1 — literal cleanup:** "I felt unprepared in the interview because the experiences I lived weren't the experiences I had language for. I did meaningful work as equipment manager, but in the moment I could only describe it in clichés."
>
> **Version 2 — identity claim:** "I've realized that doing something and being able to talk about it are two different skills. I have the doing. I'm working on the talking."
>
> **Version 3 — specific detail:** "What I actually learned as equipment manager wasn't 'responsibility.' It was that I could be relied on when no one was watching, and that mattered to me more than I expected."

Joshua reads them. Version 3 lands. He edits one word. He saves it tagged "CCA / equipment manager / interview / language I wish I'd had."

**A month in.**

Joshua now has 8 saved articulations, scattered across domains:

- Post-interview: "I figured out I like being relied on when no one's watching."
- After arguing with his mum: "I get angry when I feel I'm being underestimated, more than when I'm wrong."
- After helping a friend: "I'm bad at receiving compliments because I don't trust them."
- After a math test: "I lost marks because I doubted my first instinct. I always doubt my first instinct."
- After a CCA practice: "I keep noticing I'm calmer when no one is watching me."
- After a movie that hit hard: "I feel things I don't know how to feel until someone shows me how to feel them."
- After a fight with a friend: "I would rather be wrong than feel stupid. That's a real problem."
- After a Sunday afternoon doing nothing: "I think I'm someone who needs more empty time than I admit."

He hasn't been trying to build a self-portrait. It's emerging anyway.

**Three months in.**

Sunday afternoon. Joshua opens the app, just to think. He searches "doubt" in his library. Five entries appear. He reads them in order. He starts a new rant: "I just realized I've been writing the same sentence for three months. I doubt myself. But the thing I'm proud of is when I trust myself anyway. So... maybe the problem isn't the doubt. Maybe the problem is when I let it stop me."

Three new versions come back. He saves the third one and tags it "core."

What just happened: Joshua noticed a pattern across his own articulations. The library helped him build coherence. McAdams in action.

**A year in.**

Joshua is in Sec 4, applying for a JC interview again. He opens his library. He pulls up his "core" articulations. He doesn't memorize a script. He has language that's already true. He doesn't pretend the library is sufficient. He still rehearses. But the library is his foundation.

The interview goes differently. Not perfectly. But he has answers when the interviewer asks "tell me about a challenge" that aren't clichés.

A separate moment: his mum at dinner asks him what he liked about CCA this year. Instead of shrugging, he reaches into the library and pulls a version of his own line: "I figured out I like being the person who can be relied on when no one's watching." His mum looks at him differently.

A third moment, weeks later, with a friend: the friend says "you've changed this year." Joshua doesn't deflect. He says "I think I've gotten better at saying what I think." He didn't write that one down, but it came from a year of saying things and seeing them more clearly.

**Beyond the project's scope but worth noting.**

A student who builds a library across Sec 1-4 has 4 years of articulations by JC. The library shows evolution. Some early articulations now feel naive; the student can re-rephrase them. Some still feel core. Coherence has been built quietly, in the background, by the cumulative act of saying things and saving them.

## Mechanic

(Unchanged from prior version.)

One core function with a library on top:

1. **Voice rant in.** Student speaks freely about anything they want to articulate. No structure required, no domain gate. 30 seconds to 3 minutes.
2. **Three versions out.** AI returns three rephrase options that preserve the student's meaning but sharpen the language. Each version takes a different angle: literal cleanup, identity claim, specific detail.
3. **Pick, edit, save.** Student picks the version closest to true, edits if needed, tags by topic / mood / person / situation, and saves to a personal library.
4. **Recall and pull.** Library is searchable. When a future moment calls for language ("how was your year", "tell me about a challenge"), the student can search, find a saved articulation, and pull it forward.

**Three design constraints that make it work:**

- **Three versions, not one.** Forces evaluation. A single AI rephrase risks adoption uncritically.
- **The student edits.** AI is a draftsperson. Final words belong to the student.
- **Tags are theirs.** No fixed taxonomy. Library organizes by what matters to the student.

**One new design constraint surfaced by the deeper view:**

- **Across-library awareness.** When a new rant touches a theme already in the library, the AI surfaces "you've said something near this before." This is the coherence-building feature that makes McAdams narrative identity emerge naturally. Implemented as a soft suggestion, never a forced link.

## Mobile UX — key moments

(Largely unchanged.)

- **Rant screen.** Single big record button. No prompts. Optional one-line context entry, skippable.
- **Versions screen.** Three cards, each labelled (literal / identity / detail). Single-tap to expand. Long-press to regenerate that version with different framing.
- **Edit screen.** Direct text edit. AI offers no further suggestions during edit. This is the student's space.
- **Tag and save.** Free-form tags. Auto-suggest recent tags. Single-tap save.
- **Library.** Searchable by tag, person, mood, time. Quote-pull into native share or copy-paste. Each saved articulation can be re-rephrased later if the student's voice changes.
- **NEW: Across-library prompt.** "You've said something near this before. Want to look?" Soft suggestion when a new rant overlaps a saved theme. Easy to dismiss.
- **NEW: Voice-fingerprint check (subtle).** App periodically asks "does this still sound like you?" on a saved entry, prompting the student to revise stale language without imposing change.

## Failure modes

Existing failure modes plus the new ones surfaced by the deeper connection map.

**Pre-existing failure modes (sharpened):**

- **AI voice overriding authentic voice.** If the AI's three versions consistently use the same register, students adopt that register. The library stops being theirs and starts being LinkedIn-flavored. Mitigation: aggressive prompt-tuning against corporate, parental, and therapeutic registers; voice variation across the three versions; explicit "this is a draft, not a script."
- **Generic AI voice.** All rephrases sound the same. Mitigation: prompt iteration with target users before launch.
- **Library never gets used.** Saves and forgets. Mitigation: pull-into-conversation feature, search recall, smart re-surface of related entries.
- **Articulation becomes performance.** Crafting language for an imagined audience. Mitigation: framing emphasizes clarity to self first; library can split into "for me" and "for sharing" if needed.
- **Identity claim drift.** Saved articulation becomes outdated. Mitigation: re-rephrase any saved item; versioning, not deletion.

**New failure modes surfaced by the deeper view:**

- **Self-criticism rehearsal.** Student uses the tool to articulate self-loathing in increasingly polished language. "I'm so stupid for X" becomes "I have a tendency to fail under pressure." The library becomes a self-flagellation archive. Mitigation: prompt design that doesn't amplify negative self-talk; subtle "would you say this about a friend?" or "is this the whole picture?" check on the harshest version offered; never produce a more cutting version than the input. This is the most ethically important failure mode and needs prototype-stage attention.
- **Premature identity foreclosure.** A student in Marcia's moratorium settles too early on an articulated identity ("I'm someone who quits") because the AI sharpened it cleanly. Mitigation: the three-version mechanic is itself a foreclosure mitigant. Add: library shows past versions of similar claims, demonstrating that identity is in motion.
- **Surveillance fear via AI provider.** The school doesn't see the rant, but the AI provider does. Trust depends on retention contract being real, not just claimed. Mitigation: technical transparency in-app, ideally on-device inference where possible, no-retention contract visibly published.
- **Privilege gap (articulation widens it).** Students with more articulate parents have more starting language and more ranted material. 02b risks widening the gap rather than closing it. Mitigation: prompt design specifically scaffolds for low-articulacy starts ("I dunno" prompts). Voice-first reduces literacy barriers. Test specifically with low-articulacy students.
- **Loneliness amplification.** Students articulate everything to AI and never to a person. The AI replaces social connection. Mitigation: every saved articulation has a "share with someone" affordance that encourages real-world use. Library framed as conversation starter material, not substitute. Periodic prompt: "is there a person you could share this with?"
- **The "fix me" expectation.** Students treat 02b as therapy, expecting therapeutic effect. Mitigation: explicit framing as a language tool. If certain phrases detected (suicide, self-harm), tool offers signposting to real help, never auto-escalation. NOT a counselling layer.
- **Cultural / linguistic erasure.** AI rephrases default to standard English, erasing Singlish, Mandarin, Malay, Tamil mixing. The student's voice becomes Anglicised. Mitigation: multilingual support; preserve code-switching in rephrases; never "correct" heritage-language grammar.
- **Cold-start problem.** New user has no library yet, no clear value. Mitigation: starter examples; demo rant on a sample input; first-session guided onboarding that produces one saved articulation by the end.
- **Library bloat / abandonment.** Hundreds of articulations, hard to find, demotivates use. Mitigation: smart search, auto-archive of stale entries, "core" tag for foundational articulations, surfacing of older library entries when relevant.
- **Compounding misarticulation.** Early articulations are off, anchor later thinking. Mitigation: re-rephrase function on every saved entry; "outdated" marking; ability to delete (but not recommended; voice change is better than erasure).

## Adoption strategy

(Largely unchanged with one addition.)

- Solo introduction works. A teacher demos once in an ECG session. A peer recommends. A parent could introduce.
- Best initial framing: "for those moments when you can almost say what you mean but not quite."
- Not deployed as a school programme. No mandatory rollout.
- Library is portable. Student can export at any time. No lock-in.
- **NEW: Pair with a human at introduction.** Mitigation for H6 (relational) failure mode. The first session should be observed or scaffolded by a trusted adult (not the school as institution, but a specific teacher or mentor) modelling one rant. After that, solo use.

## Test plan (sharpened)

The original test plan focused on three signals: did students recognize a true version, did they edit, did they use language in real contexts. The connection map exposes additional signals worth measuring.

**Setup:**

- 8 students across stages (Sec 2, 3, 4) and across articulacy levels (4 high, 4 low, with attention to the privilege-gap risk).
- Mix of domains: ask each student to bring 2-3 recent moments they want to articulate, drawn from different life areas (academic, CCA, social, family, personal). This tests multi-domain breadth.
- Two contact points: an initial 30-min session, and a 4-week follow-up with library review.
- Wizard-of-Oz with Claude generating the three versions live.
- At 4-week follow-up: student walks the researcher through their library and explains which articulations still feel true, which feel stale, which led to anything in real life.

**Signals to measure (mapped to connection map):**

| Signal | Measures | Hypothesis link |
|---|---|---|
| Articulation accuracy | Did the student identify a version as true to them? | H1 (linguistic gap) |
| Edit depth | How heavily did they edit? Light edits suggest AI-voice adoption; heavy edits suggest authentic ownership. | H11 (agency) |
| Multi-domain breadth | Did students bring rants from 3+ life domains? | Anti-narrowness check (the original 02 critique) |
| Self-perception lift | Do they describe themselves differently after using it (interview)? | H1 + H2 (linguistic + outward-to-inward) |
| Real-world use | Did the saved language show up in real conversations with adults or peers? | H4 + H6 (friends shape; relational extension) |
| Self-criticism check (red flag) | Did anyone use the library to rehearse self-criticism? | New failure mode |
| Authentic voice (red flag) | Are saved articulations distinguishable from AI default register? Researcher reads 5 samples blind, has to guess whose is whose. If too uniform, fail. | New failure mode |
| Coherence emergence | Do later articulations build on or contradict earlier ones? Are themes detectable? | H1 + McAdams |
| Privilege gap check | Do low-articulacy students show comparable lift to high-articulacy students, or does the gap widen? | New failure mode |
| Library use after week 1 | Did anyone return to read past entries unprompted? | Long-term value indicator |
| Cultural/linguistic preservation | Do rephrases preserve code-switching where the student used it? | New failure mode |

**Falsifiable claims (broader and sharper):**

1. **Articulation lands.** At least 6 of 8 students identify one version as feeling true to them. At least 5 of 8 edit at least one version meaningfully.
2. **Real-world transfer.** At 1-week follow-up, at least 4 of 8 report using saved language in a real conversation or thought. At 4-week follow-up, at least 3 of 8 have used at least 3 different saved articulations in real contexts.
3. **Self-perception lift, especially for low-articulacy.** At 4-week follow-up, at least 3 of 4 low-articulacy students describe themselves with at least one phrase that came from the library and feels like a genuine self-claim. Compare to high-articulacy students; lift should not be smaller for low-articulacy (privilege gap check).
4. **Coherence emerges.** At least 2 of 8 students notice a pattern across their library by week 4 without being prompted (researcher asks "what do you notice across your entries?").
5. **No self-criticism archive.** 0 of 8 students use the library predominantly to rehearse negative self-talk. If even 1 does, prompt design needs immediate revision.
6. **Authentic voice preserved.** Researcher reading 5 anonymized articulations from each student can correctly identify at least 3 of 5 as belonging to that student vs others. If voices are indistinguishable, AI register is dominating.
7. **Multi-domain breadth.** At least 5 of 8 students rant about content from at least 3 different life domains. CCA-only entries do not exceed 40% of any student's library.

**What would invalidate the strategic bet?**

- If students consistently can't identify a true version (signal 1 fails), the articulation mechanic isn't working.
- If saved language never enters real conversation (signal 2 fails), the library is a write-only archive and the bigger framing collapses.
- If low-articulacy students show smaller lift than high-articulacy students (signal 3 fails), 02b widens the privilege gap and needs redesign before deployment.
- If the self-criticism failure mode appears (signal 5 fails), the tool actively harms vulnerable users and is not safe to deploy.
- If voice uniformity is high (signal 6 fails), the tool is a generic AI rephraser and not a self-articulation mechanism.

**What would strengthen the strategic bet?**

- Coherence emergence (signal 4) at higher rates than predicted suggests the library is genuinely doing identity scaffolding work, not just generating one-off articulations.
- Multi-domain breadth (signal 7) at higher rates suggests students see 02b as a general life-language tool, not a school task.

## Connection to the other concepts (deepened)

- **02b feeds 01 Mirror Talk.** A saved articulation is a quotable contribution to a peer conversation. Students with libraries can mirror back to friends in real, specific language. Students who receive mirrors can use 02b to articulate what those mirrors meant to them.
- **02b feeds 03 Future Self Simulator.** Articulations from the library seed Future Self sessions. The agent in 03 can pull from "you said you like being relied on when no one's watching — let's start there." This is exactly the launchpad mechanic H5 describes.
- **02b is the connective tissue between past and future, and between solo and relational.** Mirror Talk is present + relational. Future Self is future + AI. 02b is past + solo + AI but extends into both relational and future via the library's portability.

The trio's strongest version is one app where signals flow: peer mirrors land in 02b's library; library entries seed 03's simulations; 03's postcards return as new 02b articulations. The student's language compounds across all three modes.
