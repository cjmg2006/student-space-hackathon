# Research Synthesis: Student Space — Capture Layer Design (Areas 3 & 4)

This synthesis draws on educational psychology, learning sciences, narrative identity, self-disclosure, emotion regulation and institutional trust literatures to inform the capture layer of Student Space (a Singapore MOE-context platform for Sec 1–5 students). Findings are organised by sub-question. Where evidence comes from adult, clinical, or non-school populations, this is flagged. Where evidence is indirect, confidence is marked Medium or Low.

---

## AREA 3 — WHAT AND HOW TO REFLECT

### 3a. Reducing reflection fatigue and the labelling problem

**Key finding 1:** Mandated, audited reflection in school/professional settings reliably produces "safe self-evaluations" — i.e., performative, surface-level writing that meets the perceived rubric rather than authentic meaning-making.
**Evidence:** Hobson, Ashby, Malderez & Tomlinson (2009), *Teaching and Teacher Education* 25(1), 207–216, on the chilling effect of mandated reflection on novice teachers; corroborated by Coulson & Harvey (2013), *Teaching in Higher Education* 18(4), 401–413, who note that without scaffolded skill-building, "learners may be overwhelmed by the complexity and struggle to make the most of their learning experience" and produce compliance writing.
**Design implication:** Do not frame the entry surface as "Reflection." Use neutral, action-anchored framing (e.g., "Log a moment," "What happened today") and avoid rubric-visible scoring of entries — students must not be able to infer what an "A-grade" entry looks like.
**Confidence:** Medium — strong evidence base in teacher education and higher ed; less direct in Sec 1–2 populations, but consistent with general adolescent compliance findings.

**Key finding 2:** Affect labelling (putting feelings into words) acts as *implicit* emotion regulation — its therapeutic value depends on it *not* feeling like a regulatory task. When framed as a regulation task, the effect attenuates.
**Evidence:** Torre & Lieberman (2018), "Putting Feelings Into Words: Affect Labeling as Implicit Emotion Regulation," *Emotion Review* 10(2), 116–124. The authors note affect labelling "may not even feel like a regulatory process as it occurs." Lieberman et al. (2007), *Psychological Science* 18(5), 421–428, showed amygdala downregulation under incidental labelling conditions.
**Design implication:** The capture layer should let labelling happen as a side-effect of describing an event, not as the primary task. A free-text field with optional, lightweight affect tags is closer to "incidental" labelling than a forced emotion wheel.
**Confidence:** High for the underlying mechanism (multiple replications in adults and adolescents); Medium for direct adolescent generalisation in school contexts.

**Key finding 3:** "Trojan horse" framing — disguising assessment/development inside an apparently low-stakes activity — has been studied in gamification ethics and is consistently identified as a *manipulation* risk that erodes trust once detected.
**Evidence:** Kim & Werbach (2016), "More than just a game: ethical issues in gamification," *Ethics and Information Technology* 18, 157–173: "There may be situations where disclosure of the purpose of gamification could undermine its effectiveness, by sensitizing users or dissipating intrinsic [motivation]" — but the authors argue silence is ethically permissible only under narrow conditions and that discovery damages legitimacy. Pires Cabrita et al. (2025) and related work on deceptive design in children's apps document that detection of covert intent produces durable distrust.
**Design implication:** Do not embed reflection covertly inside a "game" or "social" surface. Use *transparent* low-stakes framing instead — make the developmental purpose visible but make participation low-cost, voluntary in tone, and decoupled from grades. Honest framing + low stakes ≠ Trojan horse.
**Confidence:** High for the principle that detected covert intent damages trust; Medium for the specific magnitude with Sec 1–2 students.

### 3b. Scaffolded prompts vs. open-ended entry

**Key finding 1:** With middle-school students (the closest direct analogue to Sec 1–2), *directed* prompts produce richer reflection in students with low prior knowledge or low metacognitive skill, while *generic* prompts work better for students who already have reflective capacity.
**Evidence:** Davis, E. A. (2003), "Prompting middle school science students for productive reflection: Generic and directed prompts," *The Journal of the Learning Sciences* 12(1), 91–142. Directly studied 8th graders; found generic prompts ("stop and think") helped already-reflective students integrate knowledge, while directed prompts helped weaker reflectors but risked shallow "expected-answer" responses. Ifenthaler (2012) and Belland, Walker, Kim & Lefler (2017), *Review of Educational Research* 87(2), 309–344 (meta-analysis of computer-based scaffolding) confirm: directed prompts produce performance gains but generic prompts produce more autonomous reasoning.
**Design implication:** The default capture surface should offer *both* a generic open field ("What's on your mind?") and an optional directed scaffold ("Describe a specific moment from today"). Adapt by year level: more directed for Sec 1–2, more generic from Sec 3 onward. Never force a single prompt path.
**Confidence:** High — direct, well-cited middle-school study; replicated.

**Key finding 2:** Sentence starters and structured prompts *increase quantity* of reflection but can *decrease authentic disclosure* by signalling expected answers.
**Evidence:** Linn & Eylon (2011), *Science Learning and Instruction*, summarising the KIE/WISE programme; and Ge & Land (2003), *Educational Technology Research and Development* 51(1), 21–38, on question prompts and peer interactions. Davis (2003) explicitly warns that overly directed prompts produce "expected-answer" responses lacking knowledge integration.
**Design implication:** Avoid Likert-only or multiple-choice-only entry. Where rating scales are used, place them *after* free description, not before — so students don't anchor narrative on the scale.
**Confidence:** High for the direction of the effect; Medium for the specific Singaporean ECG context.

**Key finding 3:** Gibbs' reflective cycle (Description → Feelings → Evaluation → Analysis → Conclusion → Action Plan) and the DEAL model (Describe, Examine, Articulate Learning) have been validated primarily with adults (medical students, teachers, undergraduates), not with Sec 1–2 students. Where applied in secondary settings, they tend to produce performative, formulaic writing.
**Evidence:** Hashim et al. (2023) on Gibbs in ESL teachers; Coulson & Harvey (2013) on scaffolded reflection in higher ed; Ash & Clayton (2009), *Journal of Applied Learning in Higher Education* 1, 25–48, on the DEAL model in service-learning undergraduates. **No high-quality validation of the full Gibbs cycle with 13–14 year olds was identified.**
**Design implication:** Do not adopt Gibbs/DEAL wholesale. Use a *radically simplified* sequence: Describe → (later) Articulate one learning. Defer evaluation/analysis to Sec 3+ or to teacher-mediated conversation, not the capture moment.
**Confidence:** Medium — the absence of validation with younger adolescents is a real gap; flag explicitly.

### 3c. Situation-first vs. feeling-first sequencing

**Key finding 1:** Pasupathi & Wainryb (2010) directly demonstrated a developmental gradient: children and early adolescents include disproportionately *factual* (event) information in autobiographical narratives, while *interpretive* (feeling/meaning) content rises across mid-adolescence. Asking for interpretation before facts overshoots the developmental window.
**Evidence:** Pasupathi, M., & Wainryb, C. (2010), "On telling the whole story: Facts and interpretations in autobiographical memory narratives from childhood through midadolescence," *Developmental Psychology* 46(3), 735–746. Studied participants from childhood through ~age 16. Found that fact reporting matures earlier than interpretation; interpretive capacity grows substantially across adolescence.
**Design implication:** **Direct empirical support for the working hypothesis.** For Sec 1–2 (ages 13–14), capture should default to "What happened?" before "How did you feel?" Feelings prompts should be optional, post-description, and easy to skip without consequence.
**Confidence:** High — directly relevant developmental study in a top journal, with a sample spanning the target age band.

**Key finding 2:** Habermas & Bluck (2000) and Habermas & de Silveira (2008) show that *causal and thematic coherence* — the ability to link events to self-meaning — develops late in adolescence. Temporal coherence is in place by ~age 12; causal coherence consolidates around 16; thematic ("what does this say about who I am") develops through late adolescence.
**Evidence:** Habermas, T., & Bluck, S. (2000), "Getting a life: The emergence of the life story in adolescence," *Psychological Bulletin* 126(5), 748–769. Habermas & de Silveira (2008), *Developmental Psychology* 44(3), 707–721.
**Design implication:** For lower-secondary students, do not require linkage between entries ("How does this connect to last week?"). Connection-prompting should appear progressively from Sec 3, and synthesis prompts ("What pattern do you see across these entries?") only from upper-sec or with adult-mediated review. The capture layer should *log* the linkable units; sense-making infrastructure can come later.
**Confidence:** High — foundational, well-replicated developmental work.

**Key finding 3:** Emotion differentiation/granularity is *non-linearly* developed and is in fact *low* during adolescence relative to both childhood and adulthood. Forcing emotion labelling on lower-sec students may yield noise, not signal.
**Evidence:** Nook, Sasse, Lambert, McLaughlin & Somerville (2018), "The Nonlinear Development of Emotion Differentiation: Granular Emotional Experience Is Low in Adolescence," *Psychological Science* 29(8), 1346–1357. Lennarz et al. (2018), *Cognition and Emotion* 33(2), 245–260 — adolescents who differentiated negative emotions had better wellbeing, but most adolescents *cannot* differentiate well. Kashdan, Barrett & McKnight (2015), *Current Directions in Psychological Science* 24(1), 10–16.
**Design implication:** Emotion vocabulary on the capture surface should be *minimal and visual* (e.g., a small palette of 4–6 emoji-anchored states) rather than a 20-item granular wheel. The platform's value is in capturing the *event* — emotion differentiation can be a downstream coaching goal, not a capture requirement.
**Confidence:** High for the developmental pattern; Medium for the design implication's specific magnitude.

**Key finding 4:** The Critical Incident Technique (Flanagan, 1954, *Psychological Bulletin* 51(4), 327–358) — situation-first by design — has been used successfully with adolescents in education and behaviour-change trials.
**Evidence:** Hankonen et al. (2019), *Social Science & Medicine* 232, 414–425 (Let's Move It trial with vocational-school adolescents) used CIT to identify behaviour-change triggers from event descriptions, not feeling reports. Flanagan's original work was conducted with high-school students.
**Design implication:** Frame capture around *incidents* ("Tell me about a moment from today — something that worked, something that didn't, or something that surprised you"). Incidents are concrete, observable, age-appropriate, and avoid the metacognitive demand of "how did you feel?"
**Confidence:** High for CIT with adolescents in research; Medium for daily-use platform context.

### 3d. Capture-now, interpret-later

**Key finding 1:** ESM/EMA research consistently shows that requiring extensive interpretation at capture *reduces compliance* and increases drop-out. Short, low-burden momentary capture preserves data quality; sense-making is done in analysis, not entry.
**Evidence:** van Roekel, Keijsers & Chung (2019), *Journal of Research on Adolescence* 29(3), 560–577 — review of ESM in adolescents; Silk et al. (2011), *Journal of Research on Adolescence* 21(1), 18–28; Stone & Shiffman (2002), *Annals of Behavioral Medicine* 24(3), 236–243 (foundational ESM methodology). Compliance literature consistently shows fewer items per prompt → higher completion.
**Design implication:** The capture transaction should be ≤30 seconds. Defer all categorisation, tagging, rating to an asynchronous "review" surface (weekly, fortnightly), or to a teacher conversation. The student should never feel sense-making pressure at the moment of entry.
**Confidence:** High — well-established methodological finding; directly applicable.

**Key finding 2:** Forced labelling at the point of capture imposes working-memory cost that competes with narrative production. This is consistent with the cognitive-load literature on writing (Kellogg, 2008) and with affect-labelling neuroscience (which shows the regulatory effect is larger when labelling is *separate* from active emotional engagement).
**Evidence:** Kellogg, R. T. (2008), "Training writing skills: A cognitive developmental perspective," *Journal of Writing Research* 1(1), 1–26. Burklund, Creswell, Irwin & Lieberman (2014), *Frontiers in Psychology* 5, 221, on neural mechanisms.
**Design implication:** Capture should support voice, photo, or short-text entry — modalities with lower cognitive load than structured form-filling. Interpretation prompts (themes, ratings) should appear in a separate "Looking back" surface days later.
**Confidence:** Medium — the cognitive-load mechanism is well established; the specific design extrapolation is reasoned, not directly tested in school platforms.

**Key finding 3:** Diary/journaling intervention research shows benefits accrue from *return visits* to past entries (re-reading, theming) more than from any single deep entry.
**Evidence:** Pennebaker & Smyth (2016), *Opening Up by Writing It Down* (3rd ed.); Pennebaker (2018), "Expressive Writing in Psychological Science," *Perspectives on Psychological Science* 13(2), 226–229.
**Design implication:** Build a "Look back" feature explicitly into the platform. The longitudinal value is in re-encounter and pattern recognition, not depth-per-entry.
**Confidence:** High for adults; Medium for adolescents (limited direct studies in 13–17 with platforms).

### 3e. Quantity vs. quality tradeoff

**Key finding 1:** App-based journaling studies show diminishing and conditional returns: benefits accrue only for users with *baseline reflective disposition*; high-frequency journaling without disposition produces no wellbeing gain.
**Evidence:** Smyth et al. (2018), "Writing Yourself Well: Dispositional Self-Reflection Moderates the Effect of a Smartphone App-Based Journaling Intervention," *Behaviour Change* — found benefits "only when baseline self-reflection was average or higher." Adult sample (not adolescent), so adolescent generalisation is unclear.
**Design implication:** Don't assume frequent capture = developmental gain. Pair frequency with low-friction *return surfaces* and adult-mediated conversation. Default frequency should be event-contingent (when something happens), not time-contingent (daily required).
**Confidence:** Medium — solid app study but adult sample; flag adolescent gap.

**Key finding 2:** ESM compliance and signal quality drop sharply when prompts exceed ~3–4 per day or persist beyond ~2 weeks without feedback.
**Evidence:** Russell, Gee & Liu (2024) review in *Journal of Research on Adolescence*; Heininga et al. (2024) on adolescent ESM compliance — fatigue, lower quality, drop-off.
**Design implication:** Do not push notifications more than once per day. Bias toward *student-initiated* capture. Keep the entry surface always available but never demanding.
**Confidence:** High for ESM compliance pattern.

**Key finding 3:** Identity coherence and self-concept clarity are predicted by *quality of meaning-making*, not raw frequency of self-reflection.
**Evidence:** McLean & Pratt (2006), "Life's little (and big) lessons: Identity status and meaning-making in the turning point narratives of emerging adults," *Developmental Psychology* 42(4), 714–722. McLean & Breen (2009), *Developmental Psychology* 45(3), 702–710.
**Design implication:** Optimise for *occasional rich return* (a Sec 3 student rereading a Sec 1 entry with a teacher) over daily depth. Capture is the substrate; the platform's developmental value is created in *episodic* sense-making moments, not at every entry.
**Confidence:** High for the meaning-making finding; Medium for the platform-design extrapolation.

### 3f. Teacher and peer feedback loops

**Key finding 1:** Adolescent narrative meaning-making depends critically on *responsive listeners*. Pasupathi & Hoyt (2009) demonstrated that late adolescents construct more meaning-laden, interpretive accounts when speaking to *responsive* friends, vs. more event-only narratives with unresponsive listeners.
**Evidence:** Pasupathi, M., & Hoyt, T. (2009), "The development of narrative identity in late adolescence and emergent adulthood: The continued importance of listeners," *Developmental Psychology* 45(2), 558–574.
**Design implication:** Any feedback loop needs to feel *responsive and specific*, not algorithmic. Generic "Great reflection!" pings will read as fake and may suppress disclosure. A short, specific teacher reply matters more than a high volume of automated acknowledgement.
**Confidence:** High — direct, well-cited study; Medium on direct generalisation to digital platform feedback.

**Key finding 2:** Teacher autonomy-supportive feedback predicts continued engagement and disclosure; controlling/evaluative feedback predicts withdrawal.
**Evidence:** Reeve & Cheon (2021), *Educational Psychologist* 56(1), 54–77 (autonomy-support meta-analyses); Assor, Soenens et al. (2020) on reflective inner-compass facilitation; Paulmann & Weinstein (2023), *British Journal of Educational Psychology* — autonomy-supportive *tone of voice* increased relatedness need-satisfaction and intent to self-disclose.
**Design implication:** If teachers respond, response templates should be autonomy-supportive (curious, non-evaluative, no grading). Train teachers explicitly that any reply that resembles assessment will damage the loop. Consider banning evaluative emoji ("⭐," "✅") and supporting open-ended replies only.
**Confidence:** High for the autonomy-support effect; Medium for direct platform translation.

**Key finding 3:** Peer acknowledgement triggers the adolescent reward system more strongly than adult acknowledgement does — but is also volatile and produces self-presentation pressure.
**Evidence:** Sherman, Payton, Hernandez, Greenfield & Dapretto (2016), "The Power of the Like in Adolescence: Effects of Peer Influence on Neural and Behavioral Responses to Social Media," *Psychological Science* 27(7), 1027–1035 — NAcc reward activity is robust for peer endorsement, particularly in adolescents.
**Design implication:** Peer feedback is high-reward AND high-risk. Avoid quantified peer signals (likes, counts). If peer interaction is enabled, make it *named* and *narrative* (a friend writes a sentence) rather than quantified, and make peer visibility opt-in per entry.
**Confidence:** High for the neural/behavioural finding; High for the design implication that quantified peer feedback amplifies performance.

**Key finding 4:** Performance-suppressive feedback patterns include: (a) feedback tied to grades or counsellor flags, (b) consequence-implying questions ("Have you considered talking to someone?"), (c) public acknowledgement of private content.
**Evidence:** Inferred from Hobson et al. (2009) on mandated reflection; the chilling-effects literature on student monitoring (Center for Democracy & Technology, 2021); Paulmann & Weinstein (2023) on controlling teacher voice.
**Design implication:** Teacher-visible aggregates must be separated from raw student capture. Counsellor escalation pathways must be pre-disclosed. Never surface a private entry to a third party without explicit student consent.
**Confidence:** Medium — pieces are well-evidenced, the integrated design pattern is reasoned.

## AREA 4 — SAFETY, AUTHENTICITY, INSTITUTIONAL TRUST

### 4a. Consequence fear and institutional context

**Key finding 1:** Awareness of monitoring produces a documented "chilling effect" on adolescent self-expression, particularly in school contexts. In a 2021 Center for Democracy & Technology survey, **six in ten students reported they did not feel comfortable expressing their true thoughts and feelings online if they knew their activity was being monitored**.
**Evidence:** Center for Democracy & Technology (2021–2023), "Hidden Harms: The Misleading Promise of Monitoring Students Online" and follow-up reports; Penney, J. (2022), "Understanding Chilling Effects," *Minnesota Law Review* 106, 1451–1530, on social-conformity theory of chilling effects.
**Design implication:** Make the monitoring contract *explicit and bounded*: (a) state precisely who sees what; (b) state precisely what triggers escalation; (c) provide a "private vault" tier that is genuinely not surfaced to any adult unless escalation criteria are met. Vague reassurances will not work — adolescents will assume the worst and self-censor.
**Confidence:** High for the chilling-effect direction; the 60% figure is from a self-report survey and should be cited as descriptive, not causal.

**Key finding 2:** Adolescents calibrate disclosure based on *perceived contextual integrity* — the match between the context they think they're in and where data flows. Discovering misaligned flow collapses trust.
**Evidence:** Nissenbaum, H. (2010), *Privacy in Context*; applied to adolescents in Quan-Haase & Elueze (2018), *Canadian Journal of Communication* 43(2), 281–298 ("Revisiting the Privacy Paradox on Social Media"). Wisniewski et al. (2022), "Privacy in Adolescence" (Springer Handbook chapter), summarises that teens "treat risk as a learning process" and become protective once disclosures escalate.
**Design implication:** The platform's privacy contract should be presented in adolescent-readable, scenario-based form ("If you write X, here's exactly what happens"). Test comprehension with students before launch.
**Confidence:** High.

**Key finding 3:** Restrictive parental/institutional mediation is associated with *reduced* disclosure but does not necessarily produce safer behaviour — adolescents reroute to other channels.
**Evidence:** Walrave, Vanwesenbeeck & Heirman (2012); Wisniewski, Ghosh, Xu, Rosson & Carroll (2017), *ACM Transactions on Computer-Human Interaction* — reactive/empowering mediation outperforms preventative/restrictive.
**Design implication:** Tilt toward empowerment (student controls visibility per entry) rather than blanket institutional collection. A platform that *demonstrates* respect for student agency will gain more authentic data than one that demands transparency upward.
**Confidence:** High.

### 4b. Audience effects on authenticity and depth

**Key finding 1:** Perceived audience materially shapes *what* and *how* adolescents disclose. Online experiments show that anticipating audience comments suppresses emotional content; perceiving shared experience increases cognitive processing and growth.
**Evidence:** Vitak, Liao, Subramaniam & Kumar (2018) on imagined audiences; Pingree (2007), *Communication Theory* 17(4), 439–461 (expression effects); Brody & Park (2021), "When do Online Audiences Amplify Benefits of Self-Disclosure?" — "Those anticipating comments wrote less about emotions than those who did not." (Adult sample — flag.)
**Design implication:** The default visibility should be *private to self*. Sharing should be a deliberate, per-entry act with a clear named audience (one teacher, one peer). Never default to "shared" or to ambiguous audiences. The platform should make audience visible *before* the student writes, not after.
**Confidence:** High for the audience-effect direction; Medium for the specific Sec 1–2 magnitude (the Brody & Park study is adult).

**Key finding 2:** McLean & Mansfield (2012) showed that *who* an adolescent narrates to (mother vs. friend vs. self) systematically alters the structural features of the resulting story — meaning the audience is not a filter but a co-author.
**Evidence:** McLean, K. C., & Mansfield, C. D. (2012), "The co-construction of adolescent narrative identity: Narrative processing as a function of adolescent age, gender, and maternal scaffolding," *Developmental Psychology* 48(2), 436–447.
**Design implication:** A single "share" button is wrong. Different audiences pull different stories. The capture layer should support *redirected* sharing (write privately first; later choose to send a refined version to a teacher).
**Confidence:** High.

**Key finding 3:** Default-to-private design is the correct default for the most vulnerable student. Wisniewski et al. (CHI work, 2022) and the Trust-Enabled Privacy framework (arXiv 2502.19082, 2025) show that ambiguous audience defaults push teens to disengagement and self-censorship rather than skilful boundary regulation.
**Evidence:** Wisniewski et al., *CHI Proceedings*; Trust-Enabled Privacy framework — "interactions with audiences whose trust levels are ambiguous often lead to defaulting to disengagement."
**Design implication:** Default visibility = self-only. Every escalation of audience must be a positive, informed action with a clear "who and why." Design for the most anxious student in the cohort, not the most expressive.
**Confidence:** High.

### 4c. Trust calibration and institutional transparency

**Key finding 1:** Adolescents calibrate trust *iteratively*: they probe with low-stakes disclosures and escalate based on outcomes. Trust collapses faster than it builds, and is largely irrecoverable after a perceived breach.
**Evidence:** Wisniewski et al., "Privacy in Adolescence" handbook chapter (2022); Trust-Enabled Privacy framework. Petronio's Communication Privacy Management theory (Petronio, 2002, *Boundaries of Privacy*) provides theoretical scaffolding.
**Design implication:** First two weeks of platform use will set durable expectations. Onboarding must over-deliver on transparency (concrete, scenario-based, demonstrable). Any change in data-flow policy after launch should be treated as a trust-event, not an admin update.
**Confidence:** Medium-High — strong qualitative + theoretical support; less direct quantitative work in school platforms.

**Key finding 2:** Transparency *increases* engagement under most conditions, contrary to the "Trojan horse / opacity helps" intuition. The chilling-effects literature shows that *vagueness* about monitoring is more chilling than *clarity* — students self-censor maximally when uncertain.
**Evidence:** Penney (2022), *Minnesota Law Review*; PEN America (2023) reports on student surveillance. The privacy-calculus literature (Dinev & Hart, 2006; Krasnova et al., 2010) shows that perceived control and clear benefit both predict willingness to disclose.
**Design implication:** Be explicit, not opaque. Tell students "this is for your development" — *and* tell them what the platform cannot and will not do. The "designed opacity" path (making the tool feel non-institutional) is the Trojan horse path and carries the documented trust-collapse risk.
**Confidence:** High for the principle; Medium for direct school-platform translation.

**Key finding 3:** Discovery of covert developmental tracking (the Trojan horse scenario) has not been directly tested in adolescent school platforms in the published literature — but adjacent evidence (gamification ethics, dark-pattern detection studies in children, brand-trust collapse studies) consistently shows that *post-hoc disclosure of hidden purpose* causes durable disengagement and reputational damage to the platform.
**Evidence:** Kim & Werbach (2016), *Ethics and Information Technology*; Mathur et al. (2019) on dark patterns; Pires Cabrita et al. (2025) on deceptive design awareness in children. **No direct experimental study on adolescents discovering covert reflection-tracking was found** — flagged as a gap.
**Design implication:** Treat the Trojan horse path as carrying a *systemic* risk to institutional trust in MOE, not just to the platform. The conservative path is honest framing + low stakes.
**Confidence:** Medium — adjacent evidence is strong; direct evidence on this specific scenario is absent.

## RESEARCH GAPS AND OPEN DESIGN QUESTIONS

1. **Sec 1–2 specific validation of reflection prompt structures.** Davis (2003) is on US 8th-graders in science; we lack direct studies on Singapore Sec 1–2 students reflecting on holistic/identity content. **Study needed:** A within-subjects A/B test of (situation-first vs. feeling-first) × (directed vs. generic) prompts with ~120 Sec 1–2 students, coding entries for length, specificity, and interpretive depth. **Substitutable by prototype?** Yes — a 4-week prototype with 60–80 students would generate a defensible local signal.

2. **Direct test of the Trojan horse scenario.** No published study tested adolescent reactions to discovering that an apparently low-stakes school tool was used developmentally. **Study needed:** Vignette experiment + qualitative interviews. **Substitutable by prototype?** Partially — disclosure framing can be tested in onboarding A/B, but the "discovery" scenario is harder to ethically simulate.

3. **Optimal frequency of capture for adolescents in non-clinical school contexts.** ESM compliance literature is strong but most studies are 1–2 week research projects; sustained-use platforms (months to years) are under-studied. **Study needed:** Longitudinal platform telemetry + wellbeing measures over a full term. **Substitutable by prototype?** Yes — direct.

4. **Effect of teacher reply tone on continued disclosure on a digital platform.** Autonomy-support evidence is strong in classrooms; translation to digital text-replies is reasoned but not directly tested in adolescents. **Study needed:** RCT with teachers trained on autonomy-supportive vs. neutral reply patterns. **Substitutable by prototype?** Yes — randomise teacher response style by class.

5. **Effect of peer visibility on authentic disclosure in a school-owned tool.** Sherman et al. (2016) is on social media (peer-rewarding context); a school-context platform may produce different dynamics. **Study needed:** Comparative test of self-only vs. opt-in-named-peer visibility on entry depth and disclosure rate. **Substitutable by prototype?** Yes.

6. **Whether situation-first capture actually produces "richer reflection" longitudinally.** Pasupathi & Wainryb (2010) supports the developmental claim, but the link from "fact-first capture" → "long-term identity coherence" has not been tested with a digital tool. **Study needed:** 1–2 year longitudinal cohort comparing capture sequences. **Substitutable by prototype?** Only partially — a short prototype gives signal on engagement and entry quality, not on identity coherence.

7. **Cultural/Singaporean specificity.** The narrative identity, autobiographical reasoning, and emotion differentiation literatures are Western-dominant. Singaporean adolescents in an MOE/CCE context may show different baselines for self-disclosure, audience-orientation (e.g., "kiasu," face-saving), and adult deference. **Study needed:** Local qualitative validation of the McLean/Pasupathi/Habermas frameworks with Sec 1–4 students. **Substitutable by prototype?** Partially — 1:1 student interviews during prototype testing are essential.

8. **Whether "micro-action" framing (the teacher-described effective ECG conversation pattern) produces durable behaviour change in a digital platform.** Implementation-intentions literature (Gollwitzer, 1999, *American Psychologist* 54, 493–503; Gollwitzer & Sheeran, 2006 meta-analysis, *Advances in Experimental Social Psychology* 38, 69–119, *d* = .65) supports the principle that specific if-then plans roughly double goal attainment vs. vague intentions — but adolescents in school career-planning contexts are under-studied. **Study needed:** Compare entries that conclude with no action, a vague intention, or an if-then plan; track follow-through. **Substitutable by prototype?** Yes — directly testable in-platform.

9. **The "slogans/facilitation scripts" risk.** Damon's purpose-development work (Damon, Menon & Bronk, 2003, *Applied Developmental Science* 7(3), 119–128) emphasises purpose as authentic, beyond-the-self, and pursued — not declared. Empirical work on what *kills* emerging purpose (e.g., reducing it to a school slogan) is thin. **Study needed:** Qualitative work on Singapore students' experience of values/VIPS exercises. **Substitutable by prototype?** Yes — ethnographic observation during pilot.

## SYNTHESIS: WHAT THIS MEANS FOR THE CAPTURE LAYER

Three integrated design principles emerge with high-confidence support across the literatures surveyed:

**1. Honest, low-stakes, situation-first capture.** Frame the surface around incidents, not feelings; default to private; describe purpose openly rather than disguising it. This is supported by Pasupathi & Wainryb (2010), Habermas & Bluck (2000), the affect-labelling literature (Torre & Lieberman, 2018), the chilling-effects literature, and gamification ethics work.

**2. Decouple capture from sense-making.** Logging is a 30-second act; interpretation is a separate, slower, often human-mediated surface. This is supported by ESM methodology, narrative identity research, and writing cognitive-load research.

**3. Responsive, autonomy-supportive, named feedback — not quantified peer signals.** Pasupathi & Hoyt (2009), self-determination theory work in classrooms, and the adolescent peer-reward neuroscience literature converge here.

The Trojan horse path carries a real, literature-supported risk of trust collapse and is not recommended; the conservative honest-and-bounded path is empirically safer, even if it slows initial engagement. Where the literature is thinnest — Sec 1–2 specifics, Singapore cultural context, and long-term platform effects — a small prototype test (4–8 weeks, 60–120 students, mixed-methods) can substitute for missing peer-reviewed evidence and should be sequenced before scaling.
