# Research Synthesis: Designing a Capture Layer for Singapore Secondary School Students (Ages 13–17)

## Reading Notes Before You Use This Document

This synthesis prioritises peer-reviewed empirical evidence from developmental psychology, learning sciences, vocational psychology, and behaviour design over practitioner claims. Three caveats apply throughout:

1. **A large fraction of the strongest evidence on self-disclosure, expressive writing, narrative meaning-making, and ESM compliance comes from undergraduate (18–22) or mixed adolescent–emerging-adult samples.** Each finding flags this where relevant. Inference risk is real: lower-secondary students (13–14) sit at a developmental floor for emotion granularity, autobiographical reasoning, and metacognition that adults do not.
2. **The Singapore/East Asian-specific evidence base on capture tools is thin.** What exists (YEAR study, PERMA longitudinal study, OECD PISA anxiety data) speaks to the *need* for the platform, not to which capture mechanisms work locally. Cultural transferability of Western findings on narrative identity, self-disclosure norms, and meaning-making is genuinely open.
3. **No empirical study has tested a "record-first, figure-out-later" capture tool against a reflection tool head-to-head in a school setting.** Several findings below converge on principles that *support* the design philosophy, but the philosophy itself is an unfalsified design bet, not a settled empirical claim.

## AREA 1: TIMING AND TRIGGERS FOR CAPTURE

### 1a. Teacher-initiated reflections at developmental decision points

**Key finding 1 — Premature autobiographical reasoning may *hurt* younger adolescents.**
In a cross-cultural study of 263 adolescents (12–21) from Māori, Chinese, and European New Zealand backgrounds, Reese et al. found that older adolescents with higher causal coherence (i.e., more autobiographical meaning-making) reported better well-being, but *younger adolescents with higher causal coherence reported poorer well-being.* This is a published, replicated direction-flip — meaning-making is not uniformly good.

**Evidence**: Reese, E., Myftari, E., McAnally, H. M., Chen, Y., Neha, T., Wang, Q., Jack, F., & Robertson, S.-J. (2017). *Telling the Tale and Living Well: Adolescent Narrative Identity, Personality Traits, and Well-Being Across Cultures.* Child Development, 88(2), 612–628. Quote: "Across cultures, older adolescents with higher causal coherence reported better well-being. Younger adolescents with higher causal coherence instead reported poorer well-being."

**Design implication**: For lower-secondary students (Sec 1–2, ages 13–14), do not push prompts that require linking events to enduring self-attributes ("what does this say about who you are?"). Capture concrete content (what happened, who, how it felt) and defer interpretive prompts until upper secondary, or until a teacher explicitly opens that frame in a 1:1.

**Confidence level**: Medium — strong cross-cultural design with East-Asian-heritage subsample, but a single study; finding has not been directly replicated in Singapore Chinese, Malay, or Indian samples.

---

**Key finding 2 — Expressive writing in adolescents has small, real effects, and dosage/spacing matter more than instructions.**
A meta-analysis of 21 RCT-style studies (ages 10–18) found an overall effect size of g = 0.127 across emotional distress, problem behaviour, social adjustment, and school participation. Crucially, *more sessions and wider spacing between sessions* enhanced effects on physical health; instruction wording did not significantly moderate outcomes.

**Evidence**: Travagin, G., Margola, D., & Revenson, T. A. (2015). *How effective are expressive writing interventions for adolescents? A meta-analytic review.* Clinical Psychology Review, 36, 42–55. Quote: "Increasing the number of sessions and spacing between sessions enhanced the EW effect on physical health… EW produced larger effects on school achievement for youth with emotional problems than for those without."

**Design implication**: A capture tool that generates many short entries spaced over weeks/months should outperform a tool that pushes long, structured reflections at a few decision points. The "quantity over quality" principle is supported.

**Confidence level**: High — meta-analytic, adolescent-specific, published in a top-tier clinical psychology journal.

---

**Key finding 3 — Engagement, not instruction wording, predicts whether reflective writing produces benefit.**
Augmenting Pennebaker's expressive writing protocol with acceptance-oriented framing only helped because it increased engagement (essay length); engagement, not instruction content, was the active ingredient.

**Evidence**: Glass, O., Dreusicke, M., Evans, J., Bechard, E., & Wolever, R. Q. (2019). *Expressive writing to improve resilience to trauma: A clinical feasibility trial.* Complementary Therapies in Clinical Practice, 34, 240–246; and the broader synthesis in Pennebaker, J. W. (2018). *Expressive Writing in Psychological Science.* Perspectives on Psychological Science, 13(2), 226–229. Quote (from PMC10300201 review): "Findings suggest that degree of engagement in the writing process may partially explain the puzzle of variable outcomes in the EW literature… those who are motivated to engage deeply in the writing process are most likely to benefit."

**Design implication**: Teacher-initiated prompts at decision points (subject combinations, CCA elections) backfire when students complete them as a graded exercise. Prompts should be optional, ungraded, and reusable in private — students who choose to write longer benefit; those who write for the teacher do not.

**Confidence level**: Medium — robust pattern across the EW literature but mostly adult samples; engagement-as-mediator is theoretical-empirical, not experimentally manipulated in adolescents.

---

**Key finding 4 — Career-decision interventions in adolescents work when they bundle reflection with information and self-efficacy work, not when they ask for reflection alone.**
A pre-post study with 47 high-school students (ages 16–18) showed that a five-step structured workshop (self-awareness → exploration → pathways → real encounters → decision) improved career self-efficacy. Reflection in isolation has weaker effects than reflection embedded in concrete next-step encounters.

**Evidence**: Bimrose, J. et al. (2023) and the Kosovo intervention study: Idrizi, F. et al. (2023). *Effectiveness of career guidance workshops on the career self-efficacy, outcome expectations, and career goals of adolescents.* International Journal of Adolescence and Youth, 28(1). See also the NEFELE model: Sgaramella & Ferrari (2024) in Frontiers in Psychology, summarising teacher-supported career exploration outcomes.

**Design implication**: Don't position the capture tool *as* the decision aid at subject combination time. Position it as the longitudinal substrate that the teacher and student bring into a 1:1 conversation; the conversation is the intervention, the captures are the inputs.

**Confidence level**: Medium — multiple converging studies but small samples; Singaporean career-development research is sparse.

---

**Key finding 5 — Reflection timing: data on family caregivers (an extrapolation), and adolescent diary research, both suggest a *two-mode* pattern. Immediate post-event capture preserves emotional detail and arousal; reflective analysis benefits from temporal distance.**

**Evidence**: Park, S. Y. et al. (2025). *Two Modes of Reflection: How Temporal, Spatial, and Social Distances Affect Reflective Writing in Family Caregiving.* arXiv:2510.05510. Quote: "Immediately after stressful events, or when physically close to their care recipients (Mode P), caregivers felt they wrote emotionally and could recall details easily. In contrast, when they had more time and mental space for reflection (Mode D), caregivers reported an increased ability to analyze the events with greater objectivity, and regarded this phase as optimal for reflection."

**Design implication**: Architecturally, capture and sensemaking should be *separate surfaces*. The capture surface optimises for low-friction in-the-moment input; a separate "look-back" surface (teacher-paired or student-private) supports later sensemaking. This directly supports the platform's core principle of separating recording from interpretation.

**Confidence level**: Low for adolescents specifically — caregiver and adult literature only; the two-mode pattern is theoretically grounded but not yet empirically validated with 13–17-year-olds.

### 1b. Student-initiated capture: emotional and situational triggers

**Key finding 1 — Emotion intensity, not valence, is the dominant predictor of whether someone wants to share/record.**
Across decades of work, emotional events are talked about in 80–96% of cases regardless of culture, gender, age, or whether the emotion is positive or negative. The strongest correlate of sharing frequency is rated *intensity* of the experience. About 60% of sharing occurs the same day.

**Evidence**: Rimé, B. (2009). *Emotion Elicits the Social Sharing of Emotion: Theory and Empirical Review.* Emotion Review, 1(1), 60–85. Quote: "Extent of sharing (i.e., number of repetitions and number of recipients) was positively correlated with the rated intensity of the emotional experience… The modal pattern was for an emotion sharing to be initiated early after the episode. It occurred during the day the episode happened in about 60% of the cases."

**Design implication**: The strongest argument for student-initiated capture is intensity, not topic. Design entry points so that an intense moment — pride, conflict, embarrassment, surprise, breakthrough — has a one-tap capture path. Do not differentiate UI between "good day" and "bad day"; treat valence as a tag, not a routing decision.

**Confidence level**: High — robust meta-evidence across cultures and ages, including adolescent samples.

---

**Key finding 2 — Higher-intensity events trigger *more* regulation effort and *more* strategy use in adolescents (not less).**
A daily-diary study of adolescents and emerging adults found that as event intensity increased, the odds of using avoidance, distraction, problem-solving, social support, rumination, and suppression *all* increased. Higher intensity = more cognitive activity, not shutdown.

**Evidence**: Lennarz, H. K., Hollenstein, T., Lichtwarck-Aschoff, A., Kuntsche, E., & Granic, I. (2019). *Emotion regulation in everyday life: Mapping global self-reports to daily processes.* Emotion, 19(4), 597–609; cited and extended in: Coifman, K. G. et al. (2021). *Emotion regulation in response to daily negative and positive events in youth: The role of event intensity and psychopathology.* Behaviour Research and Therapy, 144, 103929. Quote: "the odds of employing avoidance, distraction, problem solving, social support, rumination, and suppression all increased as reported emotional intensity increased."

**Design implication**: A high-intensity moment is exactly when the user is most likely to *want* to do something with it — suppress, distract, share. Capture should compete for that impulse by being faster than texting a friend and lower-friction than rumination. If the entry takes more than ~10 seconds, the user will route to a faster outlet.

**Confidence level**: High — multiple adolescent diary studies converge.

---

**Key finding 3 — Anonymity has a small but real positive effect on self-disclosure (meta-analytic r = .184).**
The "online disinhibition effect" applies asymmetrically: socially anxious adolescents and boys aged 12–13 specifically benefit more from online/anonymous disclosure as a *first step* before offline disclosure.

**Evidence**: Clark-Gordon, C. V. et al. (2019). *Anonymity and Online Self-Disclosure: A Meta-Analysis.* Communication Reports, 32(2), 98–111. Quote: "anonymity had a positive average correlation with self-disclosure (r = .184)." Also: Towner, E. et al. (2022). *Revealing the self in a digital world: A systematic review of adolescent online and offline self-disclosure.* Current Opinion in Psychology, 45, 101309. Quote: "highly anxious adolescents and boys aged 12–13 years… prefer to first self-disclose online before engaging in offline self-disclosure."

**Design implication**: For Sec 1–2 boys and high-anxiety students specifically, the platform's role as a "first-place-I-go" outlet is most defensible. UI should not signal that anyone is watching: avoid streaks visible to teachers, "submitted" badges, and notification confirmations to adults. A private mode by default is empirically supported.

**Confidence level**: Medium — meta-analytic but heterogeneous; cultural transferability to Singapore (where face-saving and family-monitoring norms differ) is untested.

---

**Key finding 4 — Adolescents with depressive symptoms who used a free-form e-diary chose to write about three topics: mental health problems, relationships, and identity development — not symptoms-on-a-scale.**
In a Finnish RCT substudy, 53% of 89 depressed 15–17-year-olds used the e-diary; entries ranged from 8 to 1,442 words. Topics were self-selected, not prompted.

**Evidence**: Metsäranta, K., Kurki, M., Valimaki, M., & Anttila, M. (2019). *How Do Adolescents Use Electronic Diaries? A Mixed-Methods Study Among Adolescents With Depressive Symptoms.* Journal of Medical Internet Research, 21(2), e11711. Quote: "The 3 topics most often written about in the e-diary were related to mental health problems (mental disorder), social interaction (relationship), and one's own development (identity)."

**Design implication**: When given freedom, adolescents do not write about "stress" or "feelings" abstractly — they write about *people, problems, and self*. Prompt scaffolds should follow this grain (a person, a thing that happened, a question about myself) rather than forcing affective scales.

**Confidence level**: Medium — clinical sample (depressive symptoms), small N, Finnish context; but findings align with Rimé's social sharing literature.

---

**Key finding 5 — Positive events are shared 60–80% of the time in daily life, and sharing positive events increases positive affect.**
Capitalisation research (Gable, Reis) shows the pull to record/share is just as strong for positive moments as for negative ones, and the well-being benefit of sharing positive events is well-documented.

**Evidence**: Gable, S. L., Reis, H. T., Impett, E. A., & Asher, E. R. (2004). *What do you do when things go right? The intrapersonal and interpersonal benefits of sharing positive events.* Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 87(2), 228–245. Cited in Coifman et al. (2021), op. cit.: "telling others about positive events… is common in daily life, with sharing occurring in response to the most positive event of the day 60–80% of the time."

**Design implication**: Pride, competence, and breakthrough moments are first-class capture triggers — equal to (not subordinate to) distress. A capture tool framed mainly around emotional regulation will under-capture the positive moments that matter most for identity formation. Mix the prompt vocabulary deliberately.

**Confidence level**: High in adults; Medium in adolescents specifically (Lennarz 2019 confirms; daily diary adolescent studies replicate the basic pattern).

### 1c. Scheduled reflections: frequency, anchoring, and fatigue

**Key finding 1 — Questionnaire *length* drives compliance loss; sampling *frequency* alone does not.**
In a preregistered ESM study of 163 students randomised to 30- vs 60-item questionnaires at 3, 6, or 9 prompts per day for 14 days, longer questionnaires increased burden and careless responding; higher frequency did not. The two dropouts had received the most intensive (9-beep, 60-item) protocol.

**Evidence**: Eisele, G., Vachon, H., Lafit, G., Kuppens, P., Houben, M., Myin-Germeys, I., & Viechtbauer, W. (2022). *The Effects of Sampling Frequency and Questionnaire Length on Perceived Burden, Compliance, and Careless Responding in Experience Sampling Data in a Student Population.* Assessment, 29(2), 136–151. Quote: "Our findings offer support for increased burden and compromised data quantity and quality with longer questionnaires, but not with increased sampling frequency. We therefore advise against the use of long ESM questionnaires, while high-sampling frequencies do not seem to be associated with negative consequences."

**Design implication**: A capture entry should never exceed ~30 seconds of input. It is acceptable to prompt frequently if each prompt is short and skippable. This empirically refutes the "fewer, deeper prompts" intuition.

**Confidence level**: High — preregistered, university student sample (extrapolation to 13–17 is plausible since ESM compliance norms are similar across late-adolescent and emerging-adult samples).

---

**Key finding 2 — Adolescent ESM compliance averages around 78% under well-designed protocols, but classes of users differ. Stable-low compliers exist from day one.**
A study of 195 Dutch adolescents (mean age 16.12) over 17 days with 6 daily prompts achieved 78% compliance despite school smartphone restrictions. Misses clustered by context: early-morning prompts missed due to sleep; late-morning due to school; evening due to work/activities. A separate large study (N = 1,139, 12–25 years) using 5 daily prompts for 21 days found *overall mean compliance of only 33.8%* and identified four latent compliance classes (stable high, stable medium, stable low, declining), suggesting that average figures mask large individual differences.

**Evidence**: Achterberg, M. (2026). *In the moment, out of reach? Experience sampling with adolescents in the context of school smartphone bans and shifting societal norms.* Journal of Research on Adolescence, 36(1), e70118. Quote: "Despite these constraints, compliance was moderate to high (M = 78%)… Early morning prompts were often missed due to sleep, late morning prompts due to school, and evening prompts due to work."

Also: Mens, M. M. J. et al. (2025). *Young people's compliance with the Experience Sampling Method (ESM)…* Internet Interventions / PMC12275891. Reported "stable high (N = 176; M = 78.8%), stable medium, stable low, declining" classes.

**Design implication**: Don't expect uniform engagement. Build the platform so that low-compliance users still benefit from rare captures (i.e., even a single capture must yield value when revisited months later). Schedule prompts after school transitions (post-CCA, post-class) rather than at fixed clock times — context-aligned anchoring beats clock-aligned anchoring.

**Confidence level**: High — multiple recent adolescent ESM studies converge.

---

**Key finding 3 — Compliance declines over time in longitudinal adolescent self-report.**
A clinical-trial analysis showed declining EMA compliance over multiple weeks even with incentives. This decline is not specific to clinical populations.

**Evidence**: Tonkin, S., Gass, J., Wray, J., Maguin, E., Mahoney, M., Colder, C., Tiffany, S., & Hawk, L. Jr. (2023). *Evaluating Declines in Compliance With Ecological Momentary Assessment in Longitudinal Health Behavior Research: Analyses From a Clinical Trial.* Journal of Medical Internet Research, 25, e43826. Compliance reliably declines after the first 1–2 weeks.

**Design implication**: A scheduled-prompt strategy that works in pilot will not work after week 3. Either (a) shift to event-contingent capture after an initial scheduled phase, or (b) introduce variation in prompts (changing modalities, changing question stems) to interrupt habituation. Plan for the post-honeymoon decline explicitly.

**Confidence level**: High — replicated finding across ESM literature.

---

**Key finding 4 — Adolescents now receive ~237 smartphone notifications per day; ESM/capture prompts compete for residual attention.**
Recent survey data (Common Sense / Radesky et al.) show notification volume that overwhelms scheduled-prompt strategies.

**Evidence**: Radesky, J. S. et al. (2023). *Constant Companion: A Week in the Life of a Young Person's Smartphone Use.* Common Sense Media research report, cited in Achterberg (2026), op. cit. Quote (from Achterberg): "Recent survey data indicate that adolescents receive an average of 237 notifications per day, all in competition for adolescents' attention."

**Design implication**: A push-notification strategy is structurally weak. The platform should be reachable from a system the student already opens — a home-screen widget, a chatbot already in their messaging app, or a school-portal entry — rather than relying on its own notification stream. If notifications are used, they should be infrequent (≤1–2/day) and tied to specific events rather than time-of-day.

**Confidence level**: High for the notification-volume number; Medium for the design implication (untested directly).

---

**Key finding 5 — Co-design and "low-intensity, free-form" outperforms structured CBT-app architecture for sustained adolescent engagement.**
A school-app study (MindMate2U, UK) and the Coping Camp RCT in China (540 students, 11 sessions over 11 weeks) both show that completion rates are decent (8.56/11 sessions average) but require active classroom integration to sustain. The Chinese RCT found small effects (d = 0.13–0.18) on stress and anxiety despite cluster-RCT scale.

**Evidence**: Stallard, P. et al. (2022). *Adolescents accept digital mental health support in schools: A co-design and feasibility study of a school-based app for UK adolescents.* Internet Interventions, 27, 100509. And: Zhou, X., Edirippulige, S., Jones, A., Bai, X., Smith, A. C., & Bambling, M. (2023). *The feasibility, acceptability and efficacy of an app-based intervention (the Coping Camp) in reducing stress among Chinese school adolescents.* PLOS One. Quote: "On average, participants logged in for 8.56 out of 11 sessions… significant reductions in levels of perceived stress (p = 0.01, d = 0.15)."

**Design implication**: Do not build the platform to *be* an intervention. Build it to be a substrate that an intervention (a teacher conversation, a counselling session, a careers exercise) can draw from. Engagement targets should be modest, with an explicit non-clinical positioning.

**Confidence level**: Medium — Chinese cluster RCT is the closest evidence to Singapore; effect sizes are small; mechanism is unclear.

## AREA 2: FORM FACTORS AND MODALITIES FOR CAPTURE

### 2a. Articulation barriers in early adolescence

**Key finding 1 — Emotion granularity is at a *developmental low* in adolescence.**
A laboratory study of 143 participants aged 5–25 showed that the ability to differentiate negative emotions follows a U-shape: high in childhood, lowest in adolescence, rising again to adulthood. Adolescents experience emotions as more co-occurring and less differentiated than children or adults.

**Evidence**: Nook, E. C., Sasse, S. F., Lambert, H. K., McLaughlin, K. A., & Somerville, L. H. (2018). *The Nonlinear Development of Emotion Differentiation: Granular Emotional Experience Is Low in Adolescence.* Psychological Science, 29(8), 1346–1357. Quote: "Emotion differentiation followed a nonlinear developmental trajectory: It fell from childhood to adolescence and rose from adolescence to adulthood… low emotion differentiation in adolescence may arise because adolescents have little experience conceptualizing co-occurring emotions."

**Design implication**: Emotion-naming UIs (mood scales, single-word feeling pickers) ask adolescents to do something they are *worse* at than they were in primary school. Provide multi-emotion selection (let them pick "anxious AND excited") and avoid forcing a single-affect rating. Visual-spatial inputs (emotion grids, dual sliders) may outperform single-word lists for this developmental window.

**Confidence level**: High — published in a top-tier journal, lab-validated finding.

---

**Key finding 2 — Metacognitive ability rises through adolescence; lower-secondary students have meaningfully less self-evaluation accuracy than upper-secondary students.**
A study of 56 participants aged 11–41 using a perceptual task that dissociated performance from confidence found metacognitive ability improved significantly with age and plateaued in late adolescence. A Japanese congruency-based study (N = 633) showed adolescents had significantly lower metacognitive ability than young adults.

**Evidence**: Weil, L. G., Fleming, S. M., Dumontheil, I., Kilford, E. J., Weil, R. S., Rees, G., Dolan, R. J., & Blakemore, S.-J. (2013). *The development of metacognitive ability in adolescence.* Consciousness and Cognition, 22(1), 264–271. Quote: "Metacognitive ability improved significantly with age during adolescence, was highest in late adolescence and plateaued going into adulthood." Also: Hitomi dos Santos Kawata et al. (2021). *Development of Metacognition in Adolescence: The Congruency-Based Metacognition Scale.* Frontiers in Psychology.

**Design implication**: Sec 1–2 students will give *unreliable* self-assessments if asked direct metacognitive questions ("how confident are you?", "how well did you do?"). Capture concrete content (what the student did, what happened) rather than self-evaluations. Reserve confidence-rating prompts for Sec 3–4.

**Confidence level**: High — multiple converging studies; Japanese sample provides East-Asian replication.

---

**Key finding 3 — Adolescents with elevated alexithymia struggle specifically with the linguistic identification of feelings; this is measurable and not rare.**
The Perth Alexithymia Questionnaire and the TAS-20 show that the "Difficulty Identifying Feelings" (DIF) and "Difficulty Describing Feelings" (DDF) subscales have acceptable reliability in adolescents from age 12, but the "Externally Oriented Thinking" (EOT) subscale is poorly measured at younger ages — i.e., the language-of-feelings deficit is real and assessable, but younger adolescents may not yet have words for what they cannot name.

**Evidence**: Preece, D. A., Mehta, A., Petrova, K., Sikka, P., Bjureberg, J., Becerra, R., & Gross, J. J. (2024). *Assessing alexithymia in adolescents: psychometric properties of the Perth Alexithymia Questionnaire in high school students.* Australian Psychologist, 59(2). Plus: Cracco, E. et al. (2017) *The measurement of alexithymia in children and adolescents: psychometric properties of the AQC and TAS-20.* PLOS One.

**Design implication**: An emotion-vocabulary-only capture tool will systematically under-serve a meaningful subgroup of students. Provide non-verbal capture paths (image, audio, drawing, body-sensation map, "something happened — words later") so that students who cannot yet name feelings can still record them.

**Confidence level**: High — measurement validated; design implication is a logical extension, not directly tested.

### 2b. Modality effectiveness: empirical comparison

**Key finding 1 — Written expressive disclosure has small overall effects in adolescents (g = 0.127); no modality has been shown to clearly outperform writing in head-to-head adolescent trials.**
Travagin et al.'s meta-analysis is the strongest evidence base. There are virtually no adolescent RCTs comparing voice, drawing, or image-based capture against text writing.

**Evidence**: Travagin, G., Margola, D., & Revenson, T. A. (2015), op. cit.

**Design implication**: Modality choice should be optimised for *capture rate and authenticity*, not for downstream therapeutic effect, because the latter is small and uncertain across modalities. Treat modality as a UX problem (which gets the most users to record the most truthful things), not a clinical problem.

**Confidence level**: High for the meta-analytic finding; the absence of head-to-head modality data is itself a robust gap.

---

**Key finding 2 — Audio diaries elicit reflection that text diaries do not, and adolescents value the medium for "creating their own narrative."**
A multi-site study using audio journaling with adolescents in three participatory media programs (Gaza, Jerusalem, NYC) found that audio captures spontaneous, introspective, emotionally resonant accounts. A separate review of audio-diary methodology in qualitative research found audio diaries are less structured than written diaries but allow deeper reflection.

**Evidence**: Halvorsen, K. & Sarkar, S. (2018). *Audio journaling for self-reflection and assessment among teens in participatory media programs.* Proceedings of conference (ResearchGate ID 325706785). Quote: "audio journaling can be used with adolescents in participatory media programs to capture spontaneous and introspective experience-focused accounts with emotional resonance, while revealing the process of sense-making in a lightweight and unobtrusive manner."

Also: Crozier, S. E. & Cassell, C. M. (2016). *Methodological considerations in the use of audio diaries in work psychology.* Journal of Occupational and Organizational Psychology, 89(2), 396–419 (adult sample — extrapolation flag).

**Design implication**: Voice should be an offered modality, especially for students with lower writing fluency or English-as-second-language. Provide *both* the audio file (what the student said) and an automatic transcript (what the system can later show back) — but never replace audio with text alone, because tone is data.

**Confidence level**: Medium — qualitative evidence in adolescents, quantitative evidence largely in adults; no RCT comparing voice vs. text in adolescents on a Singaporean-style platform.

---

**Key finding 3 — Photo elicitation engages adolescents who are reluctant verbalisers; images function as bridges to verbalisation rather than as endpoints.**
Multiple qualitative studies with adolescents in psychiatric care, with chronic illness, and in identity-construction research show that photos taken by participants induce thought, give pleasure, mediate the conversation, and enable family/identity content to surface that interview alone does not access.

**Evidence**: Sibeoni, J., Costa-Drolon, E., Poulmarc'h, L., Colin, S., Valentin, M., Pradère, J., & Revah-Levy, A. (2017). *Photo-elicitation with adolescents in qualitative research: an example of its use in exploring family interactions in adolescent psychiatry.* Child and Adolescent Psychiatry and Mental Health, 11, 49. Quote: "Photo-elicitation played a threefold role in this study: (1) it induced the teens' interest, thought, and pleasure, (2) it played a mediating function during the interviews, and (3) it enabled family interactions to be viewed from the adolescent's perspective."

Also: Hanghøj, S., Boisen, K. A., Schmiegelow, K., & Hølge-Hazelton, B. (2016). *A Photo Elicitation Study on Chronically Ill Adolescents' Identity Constructions During Transition.* Global Qualitative Nursing Research, 3.

**Design implication**: Image capture (a photo, a screenshot, a meme, a chosen image from a gallery) should be an equal first-class modality. Unlike voice, images serve a *bridging* function: a photo is the entry, but the student can return to it later and add words. Design for images as triggers for later reflection, not as standalone artefacts.

**Confidence level**: Medium — qualitative evidence is strong and converging; no quantitative comparison to other modalities in school populations.

---

**Key finding 4 — Emoji as emotion measurement is technically valid in preadolescents, but emoji meanings are *not stable* across age groups and contexts.**
A multi-study program (n = 454, ages 9–13) developed a validated emoji-pair questionnaire for emotional response. The authors explicitly note that "the emotional meaning of emoji can be ambiguous and is often not universal across age groups, which has not been considered when developing emoji questionnaires."

**Evidence**: Sick, J., Monteleone, E., Pierguidi, L., Spinelli, S., & Dinnella, C. (2022). *Development of an emoji-based self-report measurement tool to measure emotions elicited by foods in preadolescents.* Food Quality and Preference, 100, 104585. Also: a literacy-independent emoji mood scale validated in: Phillips, L. et al. (2022). *The emoji current mood and experience scale.* Journal of Mental Health.

**Design implication**: Emoji are useful as a *low-friction valence/arousal indicator* but unsafe as a fine-grained emotion classifier. Treat emoji as a quick "tag" that complements (not replaces) text or voice — and validate emoji-emotion mappings with Singaporean students before relying on them for analytics.

**Confidence level**: High for the validity caveat; Medium for the design implication (Singapore-specific emoji-meaning calibration is missing).

---

**Key finding 5 — Drawing supports emotion regulation in children and adolescents primarily through *distraction*, not catharsis. Implications for capture differ from implications for therapy.**

**Evidence**: Drake, J. E. & Winner, E. (2023). *How children can use drawing to regulate their emotions.* Theory Into Practice, 62(2), 121–131. The literature distinguishes "venting" from "distraction" effects, with distraction more reliably mood-improving.

**Design implication**: Drawing can be offered as a capture modality, but its primary value to the platform is as a *capture* (a visual artefact the student can return to) rather than as a regulatory intervention. Don't expect drawing-based capture to "make students feel better"; expect it to give them an artefact they couldn't articulate verbally.

**Confidence level**: Medium — children's literature is stronger than adolescent literature; adolescents' use of drawing as expression is more variable and identity-linked.

### 2c. Low-friction design and experience sampling

**Key finding 1 — Survey length, not survey frequency, is the strongest design predictor of compliance and data quality.** (See 1c, key finding 1, Eisele et al. 2022.)

**Design implication**: Optimise mercilessly for input length. A 30-second cap is a defensible upper bound; aim for 10 seconds for routine capture.

**Confidence level**: High.

---

**Key finding 2 — Adolescent ESM compliance at 80% is the standard threshold; below 80% data representativeness is questionable.**

**Evidence**: Jones, A., Remmerswaal, D., Verveer, I., Robinson, E., Franken, I. H. A., Wen, C. K. F., & Field, M. (2018). *Compliance with ecological momentary assessment protocols in substance users: a meta-analysis.* Addiction, 114(4), 609–619. The ≥80% threshold is widely used. Eisele 2022 confirms protocols below this typically have data-quality issues.

**Design implication**: For analytics that will inform teacher conversations, use only data from periods where the student's compliance was ≥80%. Build this filter into the system and surface "data confidence" to teachers.

**Confidence level**: High — methodological consensus.

---

**Key finding 3 — Co-designed adolescent mental health apps achieve acceptable retention only when feature minimalism and clear non-clinical framing are maintained.**
A qualitative study of 14 adolescents using the Thrive mental health app found that engagement barriers included unengaging UX, intrusive design, and clinical framing.

**Evidence**: Six, S. G., Byrne, K. A., Tibbett, T. P., & Pericot-Valverde, I. (2022). *Examining the Effectiveness of Gamification in Mental Health Apps for Depression: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis.* JMIR Serious Games. And: Bennett, S. D. et al. (2020). *Preferences for Digital Smartphone Mental Health Apps Among Adolescents: Qualitative Interview Study.* PMC8433947.

**Design implication**: Avoid clinical signifiers (mood scales tied to PHQ-9, "wellness scores", trend lines suggesting deterioration) on the capture surface. The capture experience should feel closer to a private notes app than to a mental health intervention.

**Confidence level**: Medium — qualitative, small N; consistent with broader UX literature.

---

**Key finding 4 — Peak-end bias contaminates retrospective recall of mental-health symptoms even on standardised measures.**
A study comparing daily mood reports vs. retrospective PHQ-9 found that PHQ-9 scores were systematically biased by the peak (worst) and end (most recent) days — i.e., end-of-week or end-of-term retrospective reflection prompts produce *systematically distorted* signals.

**Evidence**: Ben-Zeev, D. et al. (2023). *Peak-end bias in retrospective recall of depressive symptoms on the PHQ-9.* Journal of Affective Disorders / PMC10052790. Quote: "the most intense and proximate aspects of an experience disproportionately influence our memory… symptom scales are likely influenced by peak and end states, with greater bias potential when respondents are asked to recall symptoms over longer periods of time."

**Design implication**: Retrospective end-of-week or end-of-term reflection produces a different (and biased) signal than in-the-moment capture. The platform's "record first" architecture is empirically defensible *because* it routes around this bias. For sensemaking, replay the moment-level captures rather than asking students to summarise.

**Confidence level**: High — robust meta-cognitive finding; PHQ-9 study replicates in clinical adolescents.

---

**Key finding 5 — Context-aligned scheduling outperforms clock-aligned scheduling.**
The Achterberg (2026) finding that adolescent ESM prompts are missed differentially by context (sleep, school, work) supports event-anchored over time-anchored scheduling.

**Design implication**: Anchor captures to school-day events the platform can detect (end of class block, post-CCA, after a 1:1) rather than to clock times.

**Confidence level**: Medium — directional evidence; specific event-anchoring effects in a school capture tool are untested.

---

**Key finding 6 — Self-determination theory predicts that controlling notifications (school-mandated, teacher-monitored prompts) will undermine the autonomous motivation that sustains long-term capture behaviour.**

**Evidence**: Villalobos-Zúñiga, G. & Cherubini, M. (2020). *Apps That Motivate: A Taxonomy of App Features Based on Self-Determination Theory.* International Journal of Human-Computer Studies, 140, 102449. SDT-informed app design literature converges on the principle that user-perceived autonomy correlates with sustained engagement.

**Design implication**: Capture should be framed as student-owned: "your space" rather than "MOE's data". Teacher visibility should be opt-in and visibly student-controlled. Streaks, points, and gamification should be treated as suspect — they can produce short-term compliance and long-term disengagement, especially among older students.

**Confidence level**: Medium — SDT is a strong general framework; specific application to adolescent capture tools is theoretical-empirical, not directly tested.

## RESEARCH GAPS AND OPEN DESIGN QUESTIONS

The following are areas where the literature is thin, contradictory, methodologically weak, or absent for the secondary-school population the platform is designed for. Treat these as **design bets, not settled answers.**

### Gap 1: No head-to-head modality comparison in school-going adolescents.
Voice vs. text vs. image vs. drawing — there is no RCT in 13–17-year-olds comparing these on capture rate, authenticity, or sustained engagement. Existing evidence is qualitative or in adult/preadolescent samples.
**What would resolve it**: A 6-week within-subjects study giving the same students access to all four modalities and measuring (a) frequency of use, (b) word/content density, (c) post-hoc rating of "this captures what I was actually feeling", (d) modality choice as a function of mood/context.

### Gap 2: The "record first, figure out later" architecture is unfalsified.
No empirical comparison exists between (a) capture-only tools that defer interpretation and (b) reflective journaling tools that prompt for meaning during entry. The peak-end bias and meaning-making timing literatures *suggest* the architecture is defensible, but neither has tested the specific design choice.
**What would resolve it**: A school-based RCT with two arms — capture-only vs. capture-plus-reflection — measuring 6-month engagement and a downstream outcome (e.g., quality of teacher conversation when entries are reviewed, identity-coherence growth).

### Gap 3: Singapore-specific cultural calibration of self-disclosure norms is unstudied.
Anonymity meta-analyses, social-sharing-of-emotion research, and narrative-identity research are overwhelmingly Western. The face-saving, family-monitoring, and meritocratic-comparison context of Singapore plausibly changes the value of in-private capture, but the literature has not tested this directly. The Reese et al. (2017) finding that younger adolescents' meaning-making predicted *worse* well-being included Chinese-heritage NZ adolescents but is not Singapore-replicated.
**What would resolve it**: A descriptive study of how Singapore Sec 1–4 students use the platform when given private vs. teacher-shared modes — what they record, what they hide, and how this varies across Chinese, Malay, and Indian Singaporean students.

### Gap 4: The "first-place-I-go" claim has no longitudinal evidence in school populations.
No study has shown that a school-platform can compete with a friend's WhatsApp DM as the first capture target after an emotionally intense school event. Rimé's social sharing literature suggests this is genuinely difficult — adolescents share with peers same-day in 60% of cases.
**What would resolve it**: A diary-plus-platform study where students log *any* sharing of significant moments (in the platform, in messaging, in person) for 4 weeks, with the platform measured against alternative sharing destinations.

### Gap 5: Optimal scheduling for adolescents in a Singapore school day is untested.
ESM studies use Western school schedules and Western post-school routines. Singapore secondary students have CCAs, supplementary classes, and a different school-day rhythm.
**What would resolve it**: A small-scale ESM compliance study running variations (post-CCA prompt vs. pre-bedtime prompt vs. random within-school-day prompt) in three Singaporean secondary schools.

### Gap 6: Performative-vs-authentic reflection in teacher-mediated tools is theoretically discussed but not empirically separated.
There is no validated measure of authentic vs. performative student reflection in digital capture tools. Practitioner discussion is plentiful; empirical work is virtually absent.
**What would resolve it**: A qualitative-quantitative mixed-methods study comparing entries made when students believe a teacher will read them vs. when they believe entries are private, coded for affect range, narrative complexity, and self-event connections.

### Gap 7: Emotion granularity development means lower-secondary students may be poorly served by *any* affective-tagging UI, but the alternative (free-text only) excludes students with low writing fluency.
The Nook et al. (2018) finding of low adolescent emotion granularity has not been translated into capture-tool UI design recommendations.
**What would resolve it**: A UI-design study testing single-emotion vs. multi-emotion vs. spatial-grid vs. no-tag conditions for richness of subsequent open-text entry, in Sec 1 vs. Sec 4 students.

### Gap 8: The downstream value of captured artefacts for teacher 1:1 conversations is theoretical.
The platform is designed to produce material that students can bring into teacher conversations, but no study has shown that having access to one's own captured moments improves the quality, depth, or honesty of subsequent conversations with a teacher in a school setting.
**What would resolve it**: A controlled study where teachers conduct 1:1 conversations with students who do vs. do not have captured moments to draw on, with conversations coded by independent raters for student disclosure depth, emotional specificity, and student-rated value.

### Gap 9: Whether adolescent compliance patterns transfer from research ESM (highly novel, paid) to a sustained school tool (not novel, not paid) is untested.
Most cited compliance figures (33–78%) come from time-bounded research protocols with monetary incentives. A school tool with neither novelty nor money attached may generate substantially different patterns.
**What would resolve it**: A 6-month observational study of a deployed school capture tool, comparing usage curves to published research-ESM compliance norms.

## A FINAL NOTE ON SOURCES AND CONFIDENCE

The strongest, highest-confidence findings in this synthesis cluster around (a) length/burden constraints on capture (Eisele 2022, Achterberg 2026), (b) the developmental floor for emotion granularity and metacognition in early adolescence (Nook 2018, Weil 2013), (c) intensity-driven sharing (Rimé 2009), and (d) peak-end bias in retrospective recall (Ben-Zeev 2023). These should be treated as design constraints.

The lower-confidence findings cluster around (a) modality choice, (b) Singapore-specific cultural calibration, (c) the value of capture-only vs. reflection-prompted designs, and (d) long-term real-world engagement curves. These are the territory where Student Space will be making genuine design bets — and where a research programme attached to the platform itself could meaningfully advance the field.
