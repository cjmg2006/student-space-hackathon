# Research synthesis

Synthesis from April 2026 pen-and-paper research, school sessions (NYGH, others), and stakeholder needfinding. Use this as the grounding context for any concept ideation.

## Hypotheses

### Cluster A — How students relate to self-knowledge

**H1. The gap is linguistic, not experiential.** Students have rich enough experiences. What is missing is the prompts, language, and continuity to connect them into self-understanding.
- Evidence: Less-ready students give "very surface level responses: I feel good, I do this, without analysing why." Even high-achieving students lack "coherence." Flagged as the primary systemic gap across both school visits (Apr 21).

**H2. Students see their journey outward (pathways) more easily than inward (self).** When asked about themselves, students talk about what they do or want to do, not who they are.
- Evidence: "Students talk more about their journey (pathways) more easily than their world (internal self-understanding)." ECG stakeholder: "can't connect world of work, with who they are, with their education" (Apr 22 needfinding).

**H3. Students find it hard to see what's possible for them.** Future selves are mapped onto what is visible, what their family, friends, or school has modelled.
- Evidence: "Students from less educated families can't relate to traditional success paths." "Some are really fighting for survival, they will not be thinking so far ahead." One teacher: students from a certain school type couldn't imagine university pathways "because they can't relate to it, or see a future for themselves" (Apr 23 school session).

### Cluster B — What actually moves students

**H4. Friends shape self-knowledge more than formal inputs.** Peer relationships are the dominant channel for receiving, processing, and acting on self-knowledge, not teachers, platforms, or structured reflection.
- Evidence: "40 to 50% of student issues surface through friends rather than direct teacher contact." "Friends know themselves in ways they may not know themselves." The SASS dynamic: peers reflect back what students can't yet see.

**H5. A past experience that sparked "I'm good at this" is a powerful launchpad.** Even thin identity beliefs ("I like X", "I'm good at Y") are meaningful entry points. The intervention mines and connects, rather than creates new experiences.
- Evidence: "Experiences are indeed a powerful leaping point, even if level of maturity is: I like X, I'm good at Y" (Apr 22). Esther's example: a kid good at organising birthday parties, get to think about it as a child. Observed: a student inspired to join orientation planning, led to applying for an acting role.

**H6. Students are relational. Platform alone won't move them.** Motivation and buy-in flow through relationships first. Any tool needs to be embedded in or activated by a trusted human relationship to gain initial traction.
- Evidence: "Students nowadays are very relational." "The easiest way to get through is through relationships." "You will need personal touch on that." "Kids are very smart at getting the behavioural cues and whether the teacher really cares." Apr 23 SkillsFuture portal: "a small proportion of students took it seriously, it feels like a task." Journey Together reflection feature: near-zero usage (Apr 14 kickoff).

### Cluster D — Trust, safety, and consequences

**H9. Students trust individuals, not institutions.** Trust is built person-to-person and takes months. The trusted figure is a specific teacher, peer, or adult, not a school, platform, or role.
- Evidence: "Students only want to speak with people they're comfortable with, familiar faces preferred over professional services." "Takes time to build trust, students very reserved at start of Sec 1 or when teacher doesn't know them. More open from Term 2 onwards" (Apr 21 NYGH session). Pen-and-paper: "students trust individuals, not institutions, may not be form teacher, could be a specific teacher, other than a classmate."

**H10. Authentic sharing is suppressed by fear of consequences.** Students want to be heard but fear being referred to counsellors, parents being called, or escalation. Boundaries of who sees what need to be explicit before genuine sharing happens.
- Evidence: Student quote (Ang Yu Xian): "I said all these things and suddenly you drag me to counselling, call my parents." This creates tension that stops authentic sharing. Apr 20: "Is it really like their personal diary that happens to be on MOE server?" Stakeholder Apr 20: "As a student I'm not sure if I'll write it if it's an MOE diary." Burden of knowledge: teachers don't want to be obligated to act on everything students disclose.

**H11. Agency over the surface matters for engagement.** When students choose the form, medium, and aesthetic, engagement increases. Institutionally imposed containers reduce authenticity and uptake.
- Evidence: Teachers noted "ETC can choose whether it is a physical thing, Google doc, Notion etc., encouraged to shape it however they prefer, a surface they need to enjoy." NYGH students who maintain e-portfolios "religiously" do so for scholarship applications, a self-selected, stakes-based context. Students who feel ownership take it seriously; those who don't treat it as task completion.

### Cluster E — The action gap

**H12. Understanding self doesn't automatically produce next steps.** Self-knowledge and knowing what to try next are separate capabilities. The intervention may need to bridge both, not assume one leads to the other.
- Evidence: "Instead of figuring out I want to be a doctor, how about helping them find the next smallest thing they can try?" Teachers describe effective ECG conversations as those that move from reflection to a specific micro-action, e.g. connecting "you want to be an influencer" to "you need to learn to write scripts and understand data" (Apr 21).

## Core tensions

### Risk 1 (Critical) — Consequence fear vs authentic sharing

- Students want to be heard and want a safe space to articulate inner life. That is the core value proposition.
- Any mechanism that involves school infrastructure creates institutional surveillance risk, real or perceived, that shuts down genuine disclosure.
- **Why fatal if unresolved:** This is the load-bearing condition for everything else. If students don't believe the container is safe, no mechanism (peer, AI, teacher, gamified) produces honest signal. Ang Yu Xian's quote is the clearest evidence. The design has to credibly answer "who sees this and what happens if I say something hard" before a single student will engage genuinely. The burden of knowledge cuts both ways: teachers don't want to be obligated to act on what they see either. If unresolved, you get performative reflection at best.

### Risk 2 (High) — Trojan horse vs honesty (the covertness paradox)

- For reflection to land, it needs to arrive as something else: a game, a social moment, a creative task. If labelled as reflection, students disengage.
- Safety and trust require students know what they're sharing and where it goes. Covert design undermines the transparency that makes the container feel safe.
- **Why fatal if unresolved:** A design paradox with no easy resolution. Labelling produces fatigue and performative responses. Hiding reflective intent risks students feeling deceived when they realise the data is being used developmentally, especially in an institutional setting. The line between a well-designed trojan horse and a deceptive tool is real. If students or parents perceive it as the latter, trust collapses immediately. Need a design position on this before prototyping.

### Risk 2 (High) — Platform vs relationship (digital tool vs human conversation)

- A tool can offer consistency, continuity, low-stakes exploration, and access at any time. Things even good teachers can't reliably provide.
- Students are relational. Buy-in flows through specific trusted humans, not platforms. Previous MOE tools (SkillsFuture portal, Journey Together) failed precisely because they were standalone digital experiences.
- **Why high risk:** If you build a tool that assumes students will use it independently without a human activation layer, you replicate the Journey Together failure. The digital tool has to sit inside a relational context, triggered by a teacher, shared with a peer, or returned to because a trusted adult asked about it. This shapes the entire architecture decision.

### Risk 3 (Medium-high) — Reactive moments vs building reflective muscles

- Reflection is most potent and motivated at inflection points (subject combo, streaming, CCA leadership elections) when students have a real reason to look inward.
- Waiting for crises means you only intervene when the window is narrow and stress is high. Building continuity requires low-stakes ongoing reflection that doesn't depend on a trigger.
- **Why this matters:** If you design only for milestone moments, you miss the accumulation of signal that makes those moments meaningful. If you push ongoing reflection, you get fatigue. The resolution is probably a two-mode design, ambient and lightweight self-initiated most of the time, richer and structured at key moments. Not resolving this means your prototype will be designed for the wrong cadence.

### Risk 4 (Medium) — Teacher as essential guide vs teacher as consequence risk

- High-quality reflection still needs a skilled adult to draw out meaning. Teachers who do this well describe it as connecting dots students can't yet connect themselves.
- Teacher involvement creates institutional accountability. Students fear that opening up leads to parental contact, counselling referrals, or records that follow them.
- **Why this matters:** You can't simply route around teachers, they're the trust and activation layer. But you also can't make them the primary audience for student reflection without triggering consequence fear. The design needs to be precise about what teachers see: a holistic picture over time, not raw transcripts of vulnerable moments.

### Risk 5 (Lower, design constraint) — Institutional frame vs student-owned experience

- The tool exists within MOE infrastructure, which means it serves policy goals (LEAPS, CCE outcomes, ECG journey) and stakeholders who need to justify it institutionally.
- The moment students perceive it as "an MOE diary" or a compliance task, engagement drops. Self-driven requires students to feel it belongs to them.
- **Why this is lower but still real:** You can navigate this with framing and design, but only if you've resolved tensions 1 to 3 first. If safety, honesty, and relational conditions are met, institutional ownership becomes a background constraint rather than a fatal one. If they're not met, this becomes another reason students won't use it.

## User segmentation

Two axes: motivation to understand future self, and articulacy (ability to connect and express experiences).

| | **Low articulacy** | **High articulacy** |
|---|---|---|
| **High motivation** | **Eager but stuck.** Wants to figure out future self but lacks language and experience connections to make progress. Highest return on synthesis tools. | **Self-directed.** Already doing a version of this work independently. May not need intervention. Could be peer ambassadors. |
| **Low motivation** | **Disengaged.** Hardest to reach. Trojan horse designs matter most here. Low-friction entry, no overt reflection framing. | **Capable but apathetic.** Can articulate but doesn't see the point. Crisis or milestone may be the activation moment. |

## Implications for ideation

- **Primary segment to design for:** Eager but stuck. Highest motivation, biggest gap, most likely to validate concepts.
- **Stretch segment:** Disengaged. Test whether the concept can land without overt reflection framing.
- **Anti-pattern:** designing for Self-directed (they already cope) or assuming Capable but apathetic will be moved by reflection alone (they need stakes or relationships first).
